# Carpal Tunnel & Nerves

## Carpal Tunnel Anatomy
- Fibro-osseous space:
  - **Dorsal**: Concave volar carpal bones
  - **Volar**: Flexor retinaculum (scaphoid + trapezium tubercle → pisiform + hook of hamate)
- Contents: **Flexor tendons** (9) + **median nerve**
- Retinaculum: Dense fibrous band with slight palmar bowing
- Very little fat within tunnel; if present, only along dorsal aspect
- Tunnel narrows from proximal → distal; most constricted at **hook of hamate**

### Evaluate at Three Levels (Axial)
1. **Distal radioulnar joint** — just before nerve enters tunnel
2. **Pisiform** — proximal tunnel
3. **Hook of hamate** — distal tunnel (most constricted)

## Median Nerve

### Normal
- Volar and radial aspect of carpal tunnel, deep to retinaculum
- **Higher signal** and more **oval** than adjacent flexor tendons
- Size maintained or slightly decreases distally
- May appear flattened at hook of hamate level (normally most constricted)
- **Bifid median nerve**: Normal variant in **4-5%** of patients

### Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) {#cts}
- **Most common** compressive neuropathy of upper extremity
- Symptoms: Pain/paresthesias in thumb, index, 3rd finger, radial half of 4th finger; worse at night
- Most common cause: **Flexor tenosynovitis** from overuse
- Clinical diagnosis usually sufficient; MRI used when NCS equivocal or postoperative symptoms

### Four MRI Signs of CTS
1. **Pseudoneuroma** — focal/segmental swelling of median nerve (compare distal radius level vs pisiform; nerve should NOT enlarge distally)
2. **Flattening/angulation** — faceted nerve surface at hook of hamate level from compression against tendons/bones
3. **Bowing of flexor retinaculum** — bowing ratio: PD/TH (palmar displacement ÷ trapezium-to-hamate line); normal ≤**15%**, CTS **14-26%**
4. **Increased T2 signal** of median nerve — from obstruction of venous return → edema

### Postoperative CTS
- **Successful release**: Retinaculum severed entire length, free ends displaced volarly, tunnel contents displaced volarly
- **Failed release causes**:
  - **Incomplete release** — intact portion of retinaculum visible at some level (most common)
  - **Perineural fibrosis/scarring** — low signal scar around nerve with proximal nerve swelling
  - Persistent/recurrent mass in tunnel
  - Median nerve neuroma
- **Persistent median artery**: Can be seen splitting the nerve in carpal tunnel; important to identify before surgery

## Fibrolipomatous Hamartoma
- Benign lesion causing **profound nerve enlargement**
- **Median nerve in wrist** = most commonly affected nerve
- May be asymptomatic or cause nerve compression
- **2/3** of patients with macrodactyly (macrodystrophia lipomatosa) have this
- **Distinctive MRI**: Enlarged nerve with tubular low signal structures (nerve fascicles + fibrosis) surrounded by **fat signal**

---

## Ulnar Nerve / Guyon's Canal

### Anatomy
- Ulnar nerve, artery, and vein pass through **Guyon's canal** (ulnar tunnel)
- Floor: Flexor retinaculum + hypothenar muscles
- Roof: Layer of fascia
- Ulnar border: Pisiform and hook of hamate

### Ulnar Tunnel Syndrome
- Ulnar nerve compressed in Guyon's canal
- Causes: **Ganglion cyst**, other masses, **hook of hamate fracture**, repetitive trauma (biking, tennis, golf)
- Assess: Course, size, signal of nerve + adjacent masses on axial images

---

## Key Points
- CTS: 4 signs — pseudoneuroma, flattening, retinaculum bowing (>15%), T2 signal
- Failed carpal tunnel release: Most common = incomplete retinaculum division
- Fibrolipomatous hamartoma: Enlarged median nerve with fat + fascicle pattern; 2/3 have macrodactyly
- Guyon's canal: Hook of hamate fracture and ganglion cyst = common causes of ulnar neuropathy
- Bifid median nerve (4-5%) = normal variant
