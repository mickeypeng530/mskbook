# Intrinsic Ligaments & Carpal Instability

## Scapholunate Ligament (SL)

### Anatomy
- Horseshoe-shaped between scaphoid and lunate; best seen on **thin coronal** images
- Three portions with distinct appearances:

| Portion | Shape on Coronal | Signal | Strength |
|---------|-----------------|--------|----------|
| **Volar** | Trapezoidal | Intermediate (loose connective tissue, vascular) | Moderate |
| **Middle** | Triangular | Intermediate | Weakest |
| **Dorsal** | Band | Low, homogeneous | **Strongest** (most important for stability) |

- Volar portion attaches to cortical bone; middle/dorsal attach to hyaline cartilage or bone
- Small perforations in middle portion = common, **not clinically significant** when isolated

### Normal Variant (Pitfall)
- Intermediate signal may partially/completely traverse SL ligament in asymptomatic individuals
- **Abnormal ONLY if** signal is as bright as fluid on T2 sequences
- Signal between cartilage and ligament = avulsion only if as bright as fluid

### SL Ligament Abnormalities
MRI abnormalities indicating SL pathology:
1. **Discontinuity** with fluid signal between ligament and bone (± widened SL interval)
2. **Complete absence** of ligament
3. **Distorted morphology** — fraying, thinning, irregularity
4. **Elongation** (stretching) with widened SL joint space

- MRI accuracy: **90%** vs arthrography; **95%** vs surgery
- Partial tears more commonly affect weaker **volar** fibers

### Scapholunate Instability → DISI {#disi}
- SL ligament completely torn or stretched → scaphoid and lunate dissociate
- **Scaphoid**: Tilts volarly (rotatory subluxation)
- **Lunate**: Tilts dorsally → **Dorsal Intercalated Segmental Instability (DISI)**
- DISI = **most common** wrist instability pattern
- Diagnosed on **sagittal** MR images
- Can also occur with unstable scaphoid fracture (SL ligament intact)

### SLAC Wrist (Scapholunate Advanced Collapse) {#slac}
- End-stage of chronic SL disruption + rotatory instability
- Components:
  1. SL ligament disruption
  2. Degenerative changes between scaphoid and distal radius
  3. **Proximal migration of capitate** between scaphoid and lunate

## Lunotriquetral Ligament (LT)

### Anatomy
- Smaller and tauter than SL ligament; similar shape
- Heterogeneous low signal on coronal images
- **Stronger volar component** blends with TFC
- Harder to diagnose tears than SL (due to smaller size)

### LT Ligament Tear → VISI {#visi}
- Second most common cause of carpal instability
- Lunate tilts **volarly** → **Volar Intercalated Segmental Instability (VISI)**
- **Strong association** with peripheral TFC tears

## Extrinsic Ligaments

### Volar (Major Stabilizers)
- Stronger and thicker than dorsal; striated fascicular structures on coronal images
- **Radioscaphocapitate**: Radial styloid → across scaphoid waist (no attachment) → capitate ("seatbelt" for scaphoid)
- **Radiolunotriquetral**: Largest wrist ligament; radial styloid → lunate + triquetrum

### Dorsal
- Radioscaphoid, radiolunate, radiotriquetral ligaments
- Obliquely from distal radius to each proximal carpal row bone

### Clinical Relevance
- Injured in up to **50%** of intrinsic ligament injuries
- **Rarely surgically repaired** — not a focus in daily practice

---

## Carpal Alignment Pitfall
- Alignment on sagittal images depends on **wrist position**
- Lunate volar flexes with radial deviation; dorsiflexes with ulnar deviation
- **Normal**: Distal radius, lunate, and capitate align colinearly
- **DISI**: Lunate articular surface tilted dorsally in isolation (capitate aligned with radius)
- **VISI**: Lunate articular surface tilted volarly in isolation
- If imaged in wrist extension → lunate tips dorsally but capitate-lunate remain aligned (NOT DISI)
- **Proper positioning essential** to avoid false DISI/VISI diagnosis

---

## Key Points
- SL ligament: Dorsal portion most important; tear signal must equal fluid on T2
- DISI (most common instability) = SL disruption → scaphoid volar tilt + lunate dorsal tilt
- SLAC = end-stage SL disruption: widened interval + radioscaphoid DJD + capitate migration
- LT tear → VISI; strongly associated with TFC tear
- Wrist position affects alignment — verify proper positioning before diagnosing DISI/VISI
