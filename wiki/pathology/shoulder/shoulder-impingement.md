# Shoulder Impingement

## Definition
Compression of the rotator cuff tendons and subacromial bursa between the humeral head and the coracoacromial arch, leading to progressive tendon damage.

## Mechanism / Etiology

### External (Subacromial) Impingement
- Compression of supraspinatus tendon beneath the **coracoacromial arch**
- Coracoacromial arch = acromion + coracoacromial ligament + AC joint
- **Causes**:
  - Abnormal acromial morphology ([[bigliani-acromion]])
  - AC joint osteophytes
  - Thickened coracoacromial ligament
  - Os acromiale (unfused acromial apophysis)
  - Secondary to instability (humeral head migrates superiorly)

### Subcoracoid Impingement
- Narrowing between coracoid process and humerus
- May be congenital or post-fracture/surgery
- Affects [[rotator-cuff|subscapularis]] tendon

### Internal Impingement
- See [[posterosuperior-impingement]]

## Neer Impingement Stages
| Stage | Description | Age | Treatment |
|-------|-------------|-----|-----------|
| I | Edema and hemorrhage | < 25 | Conservative |
| II | Fibrosis and tendinitis | 25-40 | Decompression if conservative fails |
| III | Bone spurs and tendon rupture | > 40 | Surgery |

## MRI Findings
- **Subacromial/subdeltoid bursal fluid or thickening**
- **Tendon signal abnormality**: Intermediate signal (tendinosis) or fluid signal (tear)
- **Acromion morphology**: Type III (hooked) associated with impingement ([[bigliani-acromion]])
- **AC joint hypertrophy**: Osteophytes projecting inferiorly
- **Os acromiale**: Unfused acromial apophysis — best seen on axial images
- **Coracoacromial ligament thickening**
- Progressive changes: Tendinosis → partial tear → full-thickness tear ([[rotator-cuff-tear]])

## Associated Conditions
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] — consequence of chronic impingement
- [[biceps-long-head-pathology]] — vulnerable to same impingement forces
- [[adhesive-capsulitis]] — may coexist
- Secondary impingement from glenohumeral instability

## Differential Diagnosis
- [[adhesive-capsulitis]] — similar clinical presentation (pain with motion)
- Calcific tendinitis — may mimic impingement clinically
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] without impingement (traumatic)

## Pitfalls
- Os acromiale can be confused with AC joint on axial images
- Subacromial fluid alone is nonspecific — may be seen in asymptomatic patients
- Secondary impingement from instability requires treating underlying instability

## Key Points
- Impingement is a clinical-radiologic diagnosis — MRI shows the consequences
- Type III (hooked) acromion is the morphology most associated with impingement
- Progressive continuum: edema → tendinosis → partial tear → full-thickness tear
- Always assess for underlying instability as a cause of secondary impingement
