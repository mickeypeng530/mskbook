# Shoulder Avascular Necrosis

## Definition
Osteonecrosis of the humeral head — the **second most common site** of osteonecrosis after the femoral head.

## Mechanism / Etiology
- **Risk factors**:
  - Corticosteroid use
  - Marrow infiltrative disorders (e.g., sickle cell disease)
  - Fracture of surgical neck of humerus
- Most frequently involves the **superomedial** aspect of the humeral head

## MRI Findings
- **T1**: Well-defined, low signal intensity, **serpiginous line or arc** delineating the area of necrosis
- Center of lesion: Variable signal, most frequently **fat signal**
- MRI permits **early detection** before radiographic changes

## Differential Diagnosis
- Occult fracture — can appear similar but rare in superomedial location; acute fracture has abundant surrounding edema
- Bone contusion — ill-defined, reticulated edema pattern (not serpiginous)
- Subchondral insufficiency fracture

## Pitfalls
- Superomedial humeral head location is characteristic — fracture is rare here
- Acute fracture should have extensive surrounding marrow edema
- Early AVN may have subtle findings — look for serpiginous low T1 line

## Key Points
- Humeral head is the second most common site of AVN (after femoral head)
- Classic MRI: Serpiginous low T1 signal line in superomedial humeral head
- Center of lesion usually retains fat signal
- Always consider in patients on steroids or with marrow disorders
