# SLAP Lesion

## Definition
**Superior Labrum Anterior to Posterior** tear — injury to the superior [[labrum]] at the biceps anchor. Originally described by Andrews (1985) and classified by Snyder (1990).

## Mechanism / Etiology
- **Compression**: Fall on outstretched hand (FOOSH) with shoulder abducted
- **Traction**: Sudden pull on arm, overhead throwing (peel-back mechanism)
- **Repetitive overhead activity**: Pitchers, swimmers, tennis players
- Associated with [[posterosuperior-impingement]] in overhead athletes

## Classification

| Type | Description | Key Feature |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **I** | Superior labral **fraying and degeneration** | Labrum still attached; meniscoid appearance |
| **II** | Superior labral and **biceps anchor detachment** from glenoid | **Most common type**; labrum stripped from superior glenoid |
| **III** | **Bucket-handle tear** of superior labrum | Displaced fragment into joint; biceps anchor intact |
| **IV** | Bucket-handle tear extending into **biceps tendon** | Tear propagates into biceps tendon substance |

- Types I and III: Biceps anchor is **intact**
- Types II and IV: Biceps anchor is **detached/involved**

## MRI Findings
- **Best plane**: Coronal oblique
- High signal (fluid or gadolinium) at superior labrum extending between labrum and glenoid
- **Type II**: Linear high signal undercutting the biceps anchor, extending laterally
- **Type III**: Displaced labral fragment in superior joint recess
- **Type IV**: Tear extends into biceps tendon substance
- MR arthrography significantly improves detection

### Key Distinction from Normal Variants
- **SLAP tear**: Signal extends **laterally** into or away from glenoid contour ("against the flow")
- **Normal sublabral recess**: Curves **medially** with glenoid contour ("follows the flow")
- Normal undercutting cartilage: Also follows glenoid contour, lower signal than gadolinium

## Associated Conditions
- [[posterosuperior-impingement]] — in overhead athletes
- [[suprascapular-nerve-entrapment]] — paralabral cyst from SLAP tear compressing nerve
- [[biceps-long-head-pathology]] — especially Type IV
- Anterior instability in some cases

## Paralabral Cysts
- May arise from superior or posterior labral tears
- Extend into suprascapular or spinoglenoid notch
- Can compress suprascapular nerve → [[suprascapular-nerve-entrapment]]
- **T1**: Low signal, well-defined, lobular, septated
- **T2**: High signal
- Will NOT fill with gadolinium on arthrography (noncommunicating) — need T2 FS sequence to detect
- Treatment: Percutaneous drainage + corticosteroid injection, or surgical labral repair

## Differential Diagnosis
- Normal sublabral recess (follows glenoid contour)
- Normal undercutting cartilage
- [[labrum|Buford complex]] (absent anterosuperior labrum + thick MGHL)
- Myxoid degeneration of labrum

## Pitfalls
- **Sublabral recess vs. SLAP II**: Use the "follow the glenoid" rule
- Type I SLAP (fraying) may be indistinguishable from age-related degeneration
- Paralabral cysts not visible on T1 arthrographic sequences — always include T2 FS
- Magic angle effect at superior labrum can simulate pathology

## Key Points
- Type II is the most common and clinically significant SLAP lesion
- "Against the flow" = SLAP tear; "with the flow" = normal variant
- Always look for paralabral cysts when SLAP lesion is identified
- MR arthrography is the gold standard for SLAP detection
- Paralabral cysts require T2 FS sequences for detection (do not fill with gadolinium)
