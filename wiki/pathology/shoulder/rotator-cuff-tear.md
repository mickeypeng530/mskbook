# Rotator Cuff Tear

## Definition
Disruption of rotator cuff tendon fibers, ranging from partial-thickness to complete full-thickness tears. The [[rotator-cuff|supraspinatus]] is most commonly involved.

## Mechanism / Etiology
- **Extrinsic impingement**: Compression beneath the coracoacromial arch ([[shoulder-impingement]])
- **Intrinsic degeneration**: Age-related tendon degeneration, vascular watershed ("critical zone")
- **Trauma**: Acute injury, especially in younger patients
- **Predisposing factors**: Inflammatory arthritis, diabetes, long-term steroid therapy

## Classification

### By Thickness
| Type | Definition | MRI Findings |
|------|-----------|--------------|
| **Partial — articular-sided** | Disruption of joint-side fibers | High signal on articular surface; no bursal communication |
| **Partial — bursal-sided** | Disruption of bursal-side fibers | Much less common than articular-sided |
| **Partial — rim rent (PASTA/PASSTA)** | Insertional fiber avulsion from greater tuberosity | Most common partial tear (~70% of partial tears) |
| **Full-thickness** | Complete disruption from articular to bursal surface | Fluid traversing entire tendon; bursal communication |

### By Location (Supraspinatus)
- Tears usually start in the **anterior** portion of the distal supraspinatus as rim rent tears
- Propagate **posteriorly**
- Tears at the "critical zone" (1-1.5 cm proximal to insertion) are **not as common as previously believed**

## MRI Findings

### Partial-Thickness Tears
- Increased signal on fluid-sensitive sequences involving part of the tendon thickness
- **Rim rent tear**: Interruption of insertional fibers on greater tuberosity with fluid signal
  - Best detected on anterior coronal oblique images near bicipital groove
  - Confirm on sagittal oblique images
- Detection improves with joint fluid or MR arthrography
- MRI is **less sensitive** for partial than full-thickness tears

### Full-Thickness Tears
- **Direct sign**: Discontinuity of tendon with fluid signal traversing from articular to bursal surface
- On MR arthrography: Gadolinium in joint AND subacromial/subdeltoid bursa on T1W images
- **Sensitivity and specificity > 90%** for full-thickness tears

### Secondary Signs
- Fluid in subacromial/subdeltoid bursa
- Superior migration of humeral head
- Muscle atrophy / fatty infiltration (high T1 signal in muscle)
- Subacromial degenerative changes
- Rotator cuff arthropathy (severe OA of GH joint)
- **Geyser sign**: Synovial cyst extending through degenerated AC joint (massive tears)

### What to Report
1. Location of tear (which tendon)
2. Partial vs. full-thickness
3. Size of tear
4. Degree of tendon retraction
5. Quality of tendon margins
6. Muscle atrophy / fatty infiltration ([[goutallier-fatty-infiltration]])
7. Associated osseous abnormalities

## Associated Conditions
- [[shoulder-impingement]] — most common predisposing condition
- [[biceps-long-head-pathology]] — biceps torn in ~10% of supraspinatus tears; abnormal in ~33%
- [[adhesive-capsulitis]] — may coexist
- Subscapularis tear → look for [[biceps-long-head-pathology|biceps dislocation]]

## Massive Cuff Tears
- Full-thickness tears of **multiple tendons**
- Musculotendinous retraction + muscle atrophy
- Large GH joint–bursal communication
- May develop geyser sign through AC joint
- **Rotator cuff arthropathy**: Severe GH OA with osteophytes and subchondral cysts

## Differential Diagnosis
- Tendinosis (intermediate signal, no fluid-bright signal, no discontinuity)
- Magic angle effect (elevated signal on short TE only)
- [[adhesive-capsulitis]] (pain mimics impingement/cuff tear)

## Pitfalls
- **Internal rotation** obscures anterior supraspinatus rim rent tears — always check anterior coronal images near bicipital groove
- Partial volume averaging between biceps fluid and anterior supraspinatus on sagittal images
- Postoperative tendon is intermediate signal — diagnose retear only with fluid-signal gap on T2W
- Subcoracoid bursa injection during arthrography → fluid in subacromial bursa may mimic cuff tear

## Key Points
- **Rim rent tear** (PASTA/PASSTA) is the most common type of partial cuff tear (70%)
- Tears start anteriorly and propagate posteriorly
- Full-thickness tear diagnosis: fluid signal traversing entire tendon thickness
- Always assess muscle quality (fatty infiltration) for surgical planning
- MR arthrography improves detection of both partial and full-thickness tears
