# Adhesive Capsulitis (Frozen Shoulder)

## Definition
Inflammatory process causing progressive capsular thickening and retraction of the glenohumeral joint. Self-limited condition lasting 12-18 months.

## Mechanism / Etiology
- **Inflammatory** capsular fibrosis → progressive thickening and contracture
- Predisposing factors:
  - Trauma
  - Immobilization
  - Hemiplegia
  - **Diabetes mellitus**
  - Cervical disk disease
- Women more frequently affected than men

## Clinical Presentation
- Pain at rest, at night, and with motion
- Progressive limitation of **abduction and external rotation**
- May mimic [[shoulder-impingement]] and [[rotator-cuff-tear]]
- Self-limited, can wax and wane
- Duration: usually **12-18 months**

## MRI Findings
- **Key sign**: Obliteration of **subcoracoid fat** on T1W sagittal oblique images
  - Scar tissue replaces normal fat in the [[rotator-interval]]
  - Surrounds SGHL, coracohumeral ligament, and biceps tendon
  - **Highly specific** but **lacks sensitivity**
- Thickening of anterior capsular structures
- Thickened joint capsule with intermediate to increased signal on T2W
- Typically **little or no joint fluid** (distinguishes from other causes of subcoracoid fat replacement)

### Arthrographic Findings
- Decreased joint capacity
- Small capsular recesses
- Serrated appearance of capsular attachments

## Associated Conditions
- Diabetes mellitus
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] — may coexist
- [[shoulder-impingement]] — clinical overlap

## Differential Diagnosis
- [[shoulder-impingement]] — similar pain pattern
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] — pain at rest and with motion
- Large joint effusion — can also replace subcoracoid fat (but easily recognized on T2W as bright fluid)

## Pitfalls
- Subcoracoid fat obliteration is specific but not sensitive — absence does not exclude adhesive capsulitis
- Large effusion may mimic subcoracoid fat replacement — check T2 signal
- Clinical diagnosis is primary; MRI findings are supportive

## Key Points
- Look for obliteration of subcoracoid fat on sagittal oblique T1W images
- Scar tissue in the [[rotator-interval]] is the hallmark MRI finding
- Little or no joint fluid expected — presence of large effusion suggests alternative diagnosis
- Self-limited condition (12-18 months) — treat with intra-articular corticosteroid + physical therapy
