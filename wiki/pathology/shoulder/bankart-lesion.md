# Bankart Lesion and Variants

## Definition
Tear of the **anteroinferior glenoid labrum** with torn anterior scapular periosteum, typically resulting from anterior glenohumeral dislocation. The classic injury of anterior shoulder instability.

## Mechanism / Etiology
- **Anterior shoulder dislocation** — forced abduction and external rotation
- Stress on anterior band of [[glenohumeral-ligaments|inferior glenohumeral ligament]] → labral avulsion
- Anterior instability accounts for **~95%** of all shoulder instability

## Classification

### Bankart Lesion Variants

| Lesion | Description | Key Feature |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Bankart** | Anteroinferior labral tear + torn periosteum | Classic; may have bony fragment |
| **Bony Bankart** | Bankart + fracture of anteroinferior glenoid rim | Assess % articular surface involvement |
| **ALPSA** | Anterior labroligamentous periosteal sleeve avulsion | Periosteum **intact** but stripped medially |
| **Perthes** | Labral tear with intact but stripped periosteum | Labrum nondisplaced; looks normal on MRI without arthrography |
| **GLAD** | Glenolabral articular disruption | Superficial labral tear + articular cartilage defect |
| **HAGL** | Humeral avulsion of glenohumeral ligament | IGHL avulsion at humeral insertion |
| **BHAGL** | Bony HAGL | Bone fragment at humeral IGHL insertion |
| **Bennett** | Extra-articular posterior capsular avulsion | Posterior; crescent of mineralization; pitchers |

## MRI Findings

### Classic Bankart
- Detached anteroinferior labrum on axial images
- High signal (fluid or gadolinium) between labrum and glenoid
- Torn periosteum with displaced labral fragment

### Bony Bankart
- Fracture of anteroinferior glenoid
- Best on axial and sagittal oblique images
- **Report % articular surface involvement** — determines need for bone graft

### ALPSA
- Labrum displaced medially along the glenoid neck
- Periosteum intact but **stripped and displaced medially**
- May heal in medialized position → contributes to recurrent instability

### Perthes
- Labrum appears nondisplaced and may look normal
- Periosteum intact but loosely stripped
- **Easily missed** on conventional MRI — MR arthrography needed

### GLAD
- Superficial anterior labral tear (not detached)
- Associated **articular cartilage defect** on anterior glenoid
- From impaction of humeral head against glenoid during abduction/external rotation
- Patients complain of **pain rather than instability**
- Treatment: Arthroscopic debridement (no stabilization needed)

### HAGL / BHAGL
- Identified on axial, coronal, or sagittal MRI
- IGHL shows high T2 signal and/or morphologic disruption at humeral insertion
- Redundancy of residual ligament
- Ligament may be displaced inferiorly
- Often associated with subscapularis tendon tear
- Extravasation of contrast at humeral insertion during arthrography

### Bennett Lesion
- Extra-articular **posterior** capsular avulsive injury
- Most common in pitchers — from traction of posterior band of IGHL during deceleration
- **Crescent of mineralization** posterior to posterior labrum on axial images
- Low signal intensity band on MRI
- May be associated with posterior labral tear

## Associated Conditions
- **Hill-Sachs defect** — impaction fracture on posterolateral humeral head
  - Best seen on 2 most superior axial images (humeral head is normally round above coracoid level)
  - Below coracoid level, posterior flattening is normal
- Subscapularis tendon tear
- Capsular stripping/patulous capsule
- [[glenohumeral-ligaments]] injury

## Differential Diagnosis
- [[labrum|Sublabral foramen]] (normal variant — anterosuperior, reattaches at mid-portion)
- [[labrum|Buford complex]] (absent anterosuperior labrum + thick MGHL)
- MGHL mimicking displaced labral fragment

## Pitfalls
- **Perthes lesion** easily missed on conventional MRI — MR arthrography recommended
- ALPSA may look like a normal but medialized labrum
- Hill-Sachs defect: Posterior humeral flattening below coracoid level is NORMAL
- Bennett lesion mineralization may be mistaken for a loose body

## Key Points
- Bankart lesion is the hallmark of anterior shoulder instability
- Always report bony Bankart % glenoid articular surface involvement — surgical planning
- Perthes lesion is the "occult Bankart" — needs arthrography
- GLAD = pain without instability; ALPSA/Perthes = instability
- Hill-Sachs is round on superior axial images; flat posteriorly is normal below coracoid
