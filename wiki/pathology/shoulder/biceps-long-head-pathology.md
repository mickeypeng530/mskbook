# Biceps Long Head Pathology

## Definition
Pathology of the long head of the biceps tendon, including tears, degeneration, subluxation, and dislocation. The tendon originates from the superior [[labrum]] (biceps anchor), courses through the [[rotator-interval]], and exits via the bicipital groove.

## Mechanism / Etiology

### Tears / Degeneration
- **Impingement-related** (most common): Proximal to bicipital groove, in impingement zone
  - Older population
  - Associated with [[rotator-cuff-tear|supraspinatus tears]]
- **Traumatic**: Near musculotendinous junction
  - Younger population, acute severe trauma

### Subluxation / Dislocation
- Acute trauma causing disruption of:
  - **Transverse humeral ligament** (bridges lesser and greater tuberosities)
  - Usually coexists with **subscapularis tendon tear**

## Epidemiology
- Long head of biceps completely torn in **~10%** of patients with supraspinatus tears
- Abnormal (degeneration or partial tears) in **~33%** of supraspinatus tear patients

## MRI Findings

### Tears
- **Complete tear**: Empty bicipital groove on axial images — absent round low-signal tendon
- Torn tendon may retract distally
- Degeneration: Thickened or thinned tendon with intermediate signal

### Subluxation / Dislocation
- **Best plane**: Axial
- Empty bicipital groove with low-signal round tendon displaced medially
- Positions of displaced tendon:
  - **Within** a split subscapularis tendon
  - **Anterior to** subscapularis (transverse humeral ligament tear)
  - **Within the GH joint**, posterior to subscapularis (subscapularis torn from lesser tuberosity)
  - Behind the humeral head (with complete subscapularis rupture)

## Associated Conditions
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] — especially supraspinatus (10% complete biceps tear, 33% abnormal)
- [[slap-lesion]] — biceps anchor pathology
- Subscapularis tendon tear — always check when biceps is subluxated/dislocated
- [[shoulder-impingement]]

## Differential Diagnosis
- Normal biceps tendon with partial volume effect
- Biceps tendon within sublabral foramen region (normal variant)

## Pitfalls
- Biceps subluxation/dislocation → always inspect subscapularis carefully
- On sagittal images, fluid around biceps may simulate anterior supraspinatus tear (partial volume averaging)
- Dislocated biceps behind humeral head can be subtle on axial images

## Key Points
- Biceps pathology is strongly associated with rotator cuff disease
- Empty bicipital groove on axial = complete biceps tear or dislocation
- Biceps subluxation/dislocation = always associated with subscapularis and/or transverse humeral ligament disruption
- Check both axial and sagittal images for biceps evaluation
