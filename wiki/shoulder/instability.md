# Shoulder Instability

## Overview
After rotator cuff disease, instability is the major abnormality affecting the shoulder. Instability and cuff disorders **often coexist**. Instability may cause secondary impingement.

### Types
| Type | Frequency |
|------|-----------|
| **Anterior** | ~95% |
| Posterior | } ~5% |
| Multidirectional | } |
| Superior | Rare; associated with multidirectional |

### Causes
- Acute traumatic dislocation
- Chronic repetitive overhead motions (throwing) without acute injury
- Congenital: Glenoid dysplasia, medial (type III) capsular attachment, capsular/ligamentous laxity

### Clinical Classification
- **Functional**: Subjective instability; clinically stable; labral tears often present
- **Anatomic**: Subjective instability; instability on examination; recurrent subluxation/dislocation

## Anatomy: Glenohumeral Ligaments {#gh-ligaments}

The glenohumeral ligaments are **thickenings of the anterior joint capsule**. With the [[labrum]], they form the **glenohumeral labroligamentous complex**.

### Superior GHL (SGHL)
- **Origin**: Superior glenoid tubercle → **Insertion**: Superior lesser tuberosity
- **MRI**: At level of **body of coracoid** on axial; parallels coracoid
- **Function**: Prevents **inferior displacement** during abduction

### Middle GHL (MGHL)
- **Origin**: Anterosuperior labrum → **Insertion**: Medial lesser tuberosity
- **MRI**: At/below **tip of coracoid** on axial; deep to subscapularis
- Most **variable** — **absent in ~30%** (absence does NOT increase instability)
- **Pitfall**: May mimic torn anterior labral fragment on axial images

### Inferior GHL (IGHL) — Most Important
- **Origin**: Inferior 2/3 of anterior and posterior labrum → **Insertion**: Surgical neck of humerus
- **Components**: Anterior band + posterior band + axillary pouch
- **Function**: **Main stabilizer** — limits anterior translation (ABD + ER) and posterior translation (IR)
- **MRI**: Inferior glenoid on axial; well seen on sagittal oblique

### En Face View
- Three ligaments form **Z configuration** when viewed coronally

### Capsule
- Lateral attachment: Anatomic neck of humerus
- Medial attachment: Labrum or adjacent glenoid periosteum
- Three types of anterior capsular insertion:
  - Type I: On/near labrum
  - Type II: Within 1 cm medial to labrum
  - Type III: > 1 cm medial to labrum (predisposes to instability)

## Instability Lesions

### Bankart Lesion {#bankart}
- **Anteroinferior labral tear** with torn anterior scapular periosteum
- Classic injury of anterior dislocation
- May have associated **bony Bankart** fracture of anteroinferior glenoid
  - **Report % articular surface involvement** — determines need for bone graft

### ALPSA (Anterior Labroligamentous Periosteal Sleeve Avulsion)
- Bankart variant with **intact but medially stripped periosteum**
- Labrum displaced medially along glenoid neck
- May heal in medialized position → recurrent instability

### Perthes Lesion
- Labral tear with intact but loosely stripped periosteum
- Labrum **nondisplaced** — may look normal on conventional MRI
- **Easily missed** — MR arthrography needed (the "occult Bankart")

### GLAD (Glenolabral Articular Disruption)
- Superficial anterior labral tear + **articular cartilage defect** on anterior glenoid
- From impaction during ABD/ER
- **Pain rather than instability**
- Treatment: Arthroscopic debridement (no stabilization needed)

### HAGL (Humeral Avulsion of Glenohumeral Ligament)
- IGHL avulsion at **humeral** insertion (not glenoid side)
- High T2 signal and/or disruption at surgical neck insertion
- Redundancy/inferior displacement of residual ligament
- Often associated with **subscapularis tear**
- **BHAGL**: Bony humeral avulsion variant

### Bennett Lesion
- **Extra-articular posterior** capsular avulsion
- Most common in **pitchers** — traction of posterior band of IGHL during deceleration
- Crescent of **mineralization** posterior to labrum on axial images
- May progress from functional to anatomic instability if untreated

### Summary Table

| Lesion | Side | Labrum | Periosteum | Key Feature |
|--------|------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Bankart** | Anterior | Torn | Torn | Classic anterior dislocation |
| **Bony Bankart** | Anterior | Torn | Torn | + Glenoid fracture |
| **ALPSA** | Anterior | Displaced medially | Intact, stripped | Medialized labrum |
| **Perthes** | Anterior | Nondisplaced | Intact, loosely stripped | Looks normal — needs arthrography |
| **GLAD** | Anterior | Superficial tear | — | + Cartilage defect; pain, not instability |
| **HAGL/BHAGL** | Humeral | — | — | IGHL avulsion at humerus |
| **Bennett** | Posterior | ± Tear | Avulsed | Mineralization; pitchers |

## Osseous Lesions

### Hill-Sachs Defect
- Impaction fracture on **posterolateral humeral head** from anterior dislocation
- **Best seen**: Two most superior axial images (above coracoid level — humeral head is normally round here)
- **Pitfall**: Below coracoid level, posterior flattening is **normal**

### Bony Bankart Fracture
- Fracture of anteroinferior glenoid from anterior dislocation
- Best on axial and sagittal oblique
- Report % articular surface involvement → surgical planning

### Glenoid Dysplasia
- Congenital steep, retroverted, or shallow glenoid
- Reported in **~15%** of shoulders
- Absence of bony posterior lip → increased posterior labral tears
- Associated with bench-pressing heavy weights (posterior stress)

## Postoperative Instability
- FSE imaging preferred (decreased metallic susceptibility)
- Metallic/hemosiderin foci along surgical path on all sequences
- Postop labrum: Intermediate signal, may be deformed, but should be **attached to glenoid**
- Retear: Fluid/contrast between labrum and glenoid
- Look for loose or displaced surgical anchors

---

## Pitfalls
- **Perthes lesion** easily missed without arthrography
- ALPSA may look like a normal but medialized labrum
- Hill-Sachs: Posterior humeral flattening below coracoid is NORMAL
- MGHL mimics displaced labral fragment on axial
- Bennett lesion mineralization may be mistaken for loose body
- Capsular abnormalities best seen with fluid or arthrography

## Key Points
- Anterior instability = 95% of all shoulder instability
- IGHL is the most important stabilizer — injured in ABD/ER position
- Bankart = torn periosteum; ALPSA = intact periosteum stripped; Perthes = looks normal
- GLAD = pain without instability; all others = instability
- Always report bony Bankart % glenoid involvement
- Hill-Sachs: Round humeral head above coracoid; flat posteriorly below coracoid is normal
