# Shoulder Nerve Entrapment

## Suprascapular Nerve Entrapment {#suprascapular}

### Anatomy
- Courses through **suprascapular notch** → innervates supraspinatus + infraspinatus
- Continues to **spinoglenoid notch** → innervates infraspinatus only
- Provides sensory innervation to AC and GH joints

### Etiology
- **Most common**: Ganglion (paralabral) cyst from labral tear (especially [[slap-lesion]])
- Other: Large veins (varices), tumors, scapular fracture

### MRI Findings

**The mass** (ganglion cyst):
- Well-defined, round/oval, septated
- T1: Low signal; T2: High signal
- IV gadolinium: Low signal center, thin peripheral enhancement
- **Will NOT fill** with arthrographic gadolinium — need T2 FS

**Muscle involvement by location**:
| Compression Site | Muscles Affected |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| **Suprascapular notch** | Supraspinatus + infraspinatus |
| **Spinoglenoid notch** | Infraspinatus only |

**Muscle signal changes**:
| Phase | T1 | T2 |
|-------|----|----|
| Subacute (edema) | Normal | **High signal** |
| Chronic (atrophy) | **High signal** (fat) | Variable |

### Treatment
- Percutaneous drainage + corticosteroid injection
- Surgical cyst removal + labral repair

## Quadrilateral Space Syndrome {#quadrilateral}

### Anatomy
- **Boundaries**: Humerus (lateral), long head of triceps (medial), teres minor (superior), teres major (inferior)
- **Contents**: Axillary nerve + posterior humeral circumflex artery

### Etiology
- Compression of axillary nerve by fibrous bands, mass, or fracture

### Clinical
- Pain, paresthesias — lateral shoulder and posterosuperior arm
- Exacerbated by abduction and external rotation
- Weakness and atrophy of **teres minor** (invariably spares deltoid)

### MRI
- **Fatty atrophy of teres minor** — best on sagittal oblique T1W
- Speckled appearance compared to normal adjacent muscles
- May or may not identify the compressing mass

## Parsonage-Turner Syndrome (Acute Brachial Neuritis) {#parsonage-turner}

### Definition
Probably viral inflammation of peripheral nerves. Exact etiology unknown.

### Precipitating Factors
- Recent viral infection
- Recent vaccination
- General anesthesia

### Clinical Presentation
- **Sudden onset** of severe pain (no trauma)
- **Delayed weakness** in 48-72 hours
- Self-limited but prolonged — may last **1+ year**
- **Bilateral in 30%**
- Seen in ~**1%** of shoulder MRI studies

### Nerves and Muscles Affected
| Nerve | Muscles |
|-------|---------|
| Suprascapular | Supraspinatus, infraspinatus |
| Axillary | Teres minor, deltoid |
| Subscapular | Subscapularis |
| Long thoracic | Serratus anterior |

### MRI Findings
| Phase | T1 | T2 |
|-------|----|----|
| Acute/subacute | Normal | **Diffuse high signal** in nerve-distribution muscles |
| Chronic | **High signal** (fatty infiltration) | Variable |

- Pattern follows **nerve distribution**, not single tendon — distinguishes from cuff tear
- Multiple muscles simultaneously affected
- Not described in radiology literature until **fat-suppressed imaging** became routine

### Key Distinction
- MRI cannot distinguish from brachial plexus injury by imaging alone
- **Clinical presentation** is key: No trauma + sudden pain → delayed weakness 48-72h

## Differential Diagnosis Summary

| Feature | Suprascapular Entrapment | Quadrilateral Space | Parsonage-Turner |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Cause** | Mass (ganglion cyst) | Fibrous bands/mass | Viral (presumed) |
| **Muscles** | SS ± IS | Teres minor | Multiple (nerve distribution) |
| **Mass on MRI** | Yes (usually) | Sometimes | No |
| **Onset** | Gradual | Gradual | Sudden pain → delayed weakness |
| **Bilateral** | No | No | 30% |
