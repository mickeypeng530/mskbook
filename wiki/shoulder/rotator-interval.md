# Rotator Interval

## Anatomy
- **Shape**: Triangular space
- **Superior**: [[rotator-cuff|Supraspinatus]] tendon
- **Inferior**: [[rotator-cuff|Subscapularis]] tendon
- **Base**: Coracoid process
- **Apex**: Transverse humeral ligament (roof of bicipital groove)
- **Anterior border**: Capsule, SGHL, coracohumeral ligament
- **Contents**: [[biceps-long-head]] tendon
- Arthroscopic entry point for the shoulder joint

## MRI Appearance
- **Best plane**: Sagittal oblique
- Normal: Subcoracoid/retrocoracoid **fat** visible on T1W

---

## Pathology: Rotator Interval Tears
- **Causes**: Anterior GH dislocations, [[instability]], arthroscopy
- **Findings** (sagittal oblique, T2W or post-contrast T1W):
  - Patulous rotator interval
  - High signal through coracohumeral ligament and capsule
  - May communicate with subacromial/subdeltoid bursa → **mimics cuff tear**
- **Pitfall**: Distinguish from true cuff tear by intact supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons

---

## Pathology: Adhesive Capsulitis (Frozen Shoulder) {#adhesive-capsulitis}

### Definition
Inflammatory capsular fibrosis → progressive thickening and contracture. Self-limited, **12-18 months**.

### Predisposing Factors
- Trauma, immobilization, hemiplegia
- **Diabetes mellitus**
- Cervical disk disease
- Women > men

### Clinical Presentation
- Pain at rest, at night, and with motion
- Progressive limitation of **abduction and external rotation**
- Mimics [[rotator-cuff|impingement]] and cuff tears clinically

### MRI Findings
- **Key sign**: Obliteration of **subcoracoid fat** on T1W sagittal oblique
  - Scar tissue replaces fat around SGHL, coracohumeral ligament, biceps
  - **Highly specific** but **lacks sensitivity**
- Thickened joint capsule (intermediate to increased signal on T2W)
- Typically **little or no joint fluid** (distinguishes from other causes of subcoracoid fat loss)
- **Pitfall**: Large effusion can also replace subcoracoid fat — but easily recognized on T2W

### Arthrographic Findings
- Decreased joint capacity
- Small capsular recesses
- Serrated capsular attachments

### Treatment
- Intra-articular corticosteroid injection + physical therapy
