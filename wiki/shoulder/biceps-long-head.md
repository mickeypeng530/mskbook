# Biceps Long Head

## Anatomy
- **Origin**: Superior [[labrum]] (biceps anchor)
- **Course**: Through [[rotator-interval]] → bicipital groove (held by transverse humeral ligament)
- **Best planes**: Sagittal oblique (proximal, cross section); axial (in groove, cross section); far anterior coronal oblique (at anchor)

## Pathology: Tears / Degeneration

### Impingement-Related (Common)
- Proximal to bicipital groove, in impingement zone
- Older population
- Associated with [[rotator-cuff]] tears:
  - Completely torn in **~10%** of supraspinatus tears
  - Abnormal (degeneration/partial tear) in **~33%**

### Traumatic (Less Common)
- Near musculotendinous junction
- Younger population, acute severe trauma

### MRI: Complete Tear
- **Empty bicipital groove** on axial — absent round low-signal tendon
- Torn tendon may retract distally

## Pathology: Subluxation / Dislocation

Requires disruption of:
- **Transverse humeral ligament**
- Usually coexists with **subscapularis tendon tear**

### Positions of Displaced Tendon (Axial MRI)
| Position | Associated Injury |
|----------|------------------|
| Within split subscapularis | Subscapularis partial tear |
| Anterior to subscapularis | Transverse humeral ligament tear |
| Within GH joint, posterior to subscapularis | Subscapularis torn from lesser tuberosity |
| Behind humeral head | Complete subscapularis rupture |

## Pitfalls
- Biceps subluxation/dislocation → **always inspect subscapularis**
- Fluid around biceps on sagittal may simulate anterior supraspinatus tear (partial volume)
- Dislocated biceps behind humeral head can be subtle

## Key Points
- Empty bicipital groove = complete tear or dislocation
- Biceps displacement = always subscapularis and/or transverse humeral ligament disruption
- Strongly associated with rotator cuff disease
