# Collateral Ligaments

## Medial Collateral Ligament (MCL)

### Anatomy
- Origin: Medial distal femur → Insertion: Medial proximal tibia (several cm below joint line)
- Deep fibers interlaced with joint capsule; medial meniscus attached directly to it
- **Not intrasynovial** — not seen or repaired arthroscopically
- MRI is highly accurate for MCL evaluation

### MCL Injury Grading {#mcl-grades}

| Grade | Type | MRI Finding (Fluid-Sensitive Coronal) |
|-------|------|---------------------------------------|
| **I** | Sprain | High signal in soft tissues **along** MCL; ligament intact |
| **II** | Partial tear | High signal in adjacent soft tissues + **within** MCL; partial fiber disruption |
| **III** | Complete tear | Complete **disruption** of MCL |

- Grade I and II: Conservative treatment — bracing, continue athletic activities as tolerated
- Grade III: Seldom repaired unless **multiple ligaments** also torn
- **Pitfall**: High signal medial to MCL can also be from meniscal tear or medial compartment OA — not necessarily MCL sprain

### Meniscocapsular Separation
- Fluid between MCL and medial meniscus on **fluid-sensitive coronal** images
- Easily overlooked on non-fluid-sensitive sequences
- Clinically identical to MCL sprain → patients often incorrectly braced and allowed to play
- **This is UNACCEPTABLE treatment** — the vascular interface can become avascular with continued activity
- Treatment: **Immobilization or surgical repair** (NOT brace and play)
- If isolated to MCL area → consider partial tear of MCL deep fibers
- Short segment separation = likely insignificant
- Extension to **posterior oblique ligament** = more significant for stability

## Lateral Collateral Ligament (LCL) Complex

### Components (Posterior to Anterior)
1. **Biceps femoris tendon** — inserts onto proximal fibula (conjoined with LCL)
2. **LCL** — inserts onto proximal fibula (conjoined with biceps)
3. **Iliotibial band** — inserts onto **Gerdy's tubercle** (anterolateral tibia)

### LCL Tear
- Less common than MCL tears
- Often associated with injury to other ligaments and **posterolateral corner** structures

---

## Posterolateral Corner (PLC) {#plc}

### Key Structures
- **Arcuate ligament**: Y-shaped; fibular styloid process → lateral femoral condyle + lateral capsule
  - Disruption of lateral capsule at joint line = reliable sign of arcuate ligament tear (axial images)
- **Popliteofibular ligament**: One of the strongest lateral stabilizers
  - Find on coronal: Just beneath **lateral geniculate vessels**
  - Find on sagittal: Superficial to popliteus tendon, inserting onto fibula

### Posterolateral Corner Injury
- **Definition**: LCL component injury + tears of popliteus tendon, arcuate ligament, popliteofibular ligament + either ACL or PCL tear
- Results in pain and instability with **knee hyperextension**
- **Near-surgical emergency** — failure to treat within **10-14 days** → high incidence of poor outcome
- → **Notify referring clinician promptly**

### Popliteus Tendon Tear
- Usually at musculotendinous junction; rarely isolated (usually with PLC injury or knee dislocation)
- MRI: Large fluid in popliteus tendon sheath, **lax/wavy** tendon (not taut), high signal in/around popliteus muscle

---

## Iliotibial Band Syndrome {#itb-syndrome}

- Common cause of **anterolateral knee pain** in runners
- Mechanism: ITB rubbing on lateral femoral condyle (friction)
- MRI: Fluid on **both sides** of ITB (best seen on axial images)
  - Early: Fluid/edema only **deep** to ITB (hard to distinguish from joint effusion extending posterolaterally)
  - If no joint effusion → edema between ITB and femur is reliable indicator
- ITB may be slightly thickened with high intrasubstance signal
- Can be confused clinically with **lateral meniscus tear** — imaging avoids unnecessary surgery

---

## Key Points
- MCL Grade I-II = conservative; Grade III = repair only if multi-ligament injury
- Meniscocapsular separation mimics MCL sprain clinically — DO NOT brace and play; immobilize or repair
- Posterolateral corner injury = surgical near-emergency (10-14 days window)
- Lateral capsule disruption at joint line → arcuate ligament torn
- ITB syndrome: Fluid around ITB on axial; clinically mimics lateral meniscus tear
