# Patellofemoral Joint

## Patellar Dislocation

### Mechanism
- Patella dislocates laterally → often **spontaneously reduces**
- ~50% of patients are unaware of what occurred — referred as "rule out internal derangement"
- Predisposing factors: **Shallow trochlear groove**, **patella alta**

### MRI Findings
- **Characteristic bone contusion pattern**:
  - Anterior **lateral femoral condyle** (impaction during dislocation/reduction)
  - ± Kissing contusion on **medial patella** (may or may not be present)
- **Medial retinaculum**: Always injured; frank tear may be hard to appreciate
- **Articular cartilage**: Carefully examine patella and lateral femoral condyle
  - Missing cartilage fragment → usually requires **arthroscopy**
  - Normal cartilage → usually treated **nonsurgically**
  - **Radiologist's main role**: Assess cartilage status for surgical decision

## Synovial Plicae

- Remnants of fetal compartmental folds that fail to completely regress
- **> 50%** of normal knees show one or more plicae on MRI
- Three common plicae: Superior, inferior, and medial patellar

### Medial Patellar Plica
- Thin fibrous band from **medial capsule toward medial patellar facet**
- Best seen on **axial and sagittal** images
- Can become thickened, stiff, trapped between patella and femur
- Symptoms: Pain, clicking, locking — mimics **torn meniscus**
- No measurement criteria — diagnosed by experience (when it appears "too thick")
- **No correlation** between plica appearance and clinical symptoms
- Easily removed at arthroscopy; uncommon diagnosis
- Look for it when medial joint line symptoms + no meniscal pathology

### Suprapatellar Plica
- Best seen on **sagittal** — courses through suprapatellar recess
- May be imperforate → divides suprapatellar pouch into separate compartment
- Rarely, PVNS, synovial chondromatosis, or loculated effusion in this space can mimic a suprapatellar mass/tumor

### Infrapatellar Plica
- From inferior pole of patella → through Hoffa's fat pad → transverse ligament → anterior to ACL
- Can become thickened and irritated in athletes → **anterior knee pain**
- MRI: Increased T2 signal along its course in Hoffa's fat pad
- Easily resected arthroscopically with pain relief

## Patellar Tendon (Jumper's Knee)

- Infrapatellar pain in athletes
- MRI: Thickening of **proximal patellar tendon** with high signal in and around it on T2W
- Can be debilitating; may require surgery to remove focus of **myxoid degeneration**

---

## Fat Pad Impingement

- Common source of patellofemoral pain (~**12%** of cases)
- **Hoffa's fat pad**: Increased T2 signal just **inferior to patella**
  - Impingement by patellar tendon on femoral condyle during flexion
- **Suprapatellar fat pad**: Increased T2 signal superior to patella
  - Impingement by quadriceps tendon during flexion

---

## Key Points
- Patellar dislocation: Lateral femoral condyle + medial patella contusions; check cartilage for surgical decision
- Medial plica: Mimics meniscal tear clinically; no reliable imaging criteria for symptoms
- Infrapatellar plica: T2 signal in Hoffa's fat pad in athletes with anterior knee pain
- Jumper's knee: Proximal patellar tendon thickening + high signal
- Fat pad impingement: T2 signal in Hoffa's or suprapatellar fat pad (~12%)
