# Cruciate Ligaments

## Anterior Cruciate Ligament (ACL)

### Anatomy
- Straight, taut fibers running **parallel to the roof of the intercondylar notch**
- Normal appearance: Striated with some high signal, especially at **tibial insertion**
- Best evaluated on **T2W sagittal** images; routinely check all three planes

### MRI Accuracy
- Approaching **95-100%** in almost all reported series

### ACL Tear
- Usually obvious — no normal-appearing fibers identified; midsubstance "explosion"
- **Subtle tear**: Fibers appear intact but angle is **flatter than normal** (not parallel to notch roof)
  - Check origin on axial and coronal — oval low signal structure should be firmly attached to lateral wall of notch
- **Partial tear**: Some intact fibers with more high signal than usual elsewhere in the ligament
  - Difficult clinical diagnosis — knee usually stable on exam → MRI plays important role

### ACL Ganglion (Pitfall)
- Cysts of unknown etiology within the ligament
- **Drumstick appearance** on sagittal; fluid signal within ACL on coronal/axial
- Normal fibers present but thinned by mass effect
- Patient is **stable on exam**, usually asymptomatic (may feel fullness, limited flexion)
- Do NOT confuse with ACL tear or tumor

### Postoperative ACL

#### Graft Evaluation
- Graft (patellar tendon or hamstrings) should be **taut** with some T2 high signal
  - Increased signal normal within first **18 months** after reconstruction
- Disrupted or absent graft = **failed**
- **Tibial tunnel**: Should be parallel to and in line with roof of intercondylar notch
  - Too steep/anterior → graft impinged by femur
  - Too flat → graft too lax, insufficient stability

#### Cyclops Lesion
- Most common cause of pain after ACL arthroscopy
- **Arthrofibrosis** (scar) in Hoffa's fat pad and along anterior margin of graft
- Patient presents with progressive **loss of extension**
- MRI: Round, lobular mass of scar tissue anterior to graft and/or in Hoffa's fat pad
- Often requires resection

#### Arthrofibrosis Band
- Linear band of scar tissue extending to inferior pole of patella
- Can restrict patellar motion and cause pain

## Posterior Cruciate Ligament (PCL)

### Anatomy
- Low signal structure in **medial intercondylar notch**
- Gently curving between posterior femur and tibia on sagittal images

### PCL Tear
- Infrequently torn; even less frequently reconstructed
- **Unique mechanism**: May NOT show frank disruption — instead **stretches** and becomes structurally incompetent ("overstretched elastic")
- MRI findings:
  - **Stretching injury**: Enlarged ligament with gray intrasubstance signal on short TE
  - **Typical tear**: High signal disruption on T2W images
  - **Avulsion** from tibial attachment: Easily diagnosed but much less common
- Chronic tears difficult to distinguish from acute without clinical history
- In most cases, torn PCL is **not surgically repaired**

---

## Key Points
- ACL: Parallel to notch roof = normal; flatter angle = torn
- ACL ganglion: Drumstick shape, stable knee — not a tear, not a tumor
- Postop ACL: Taut graft = intact; cyclops lesion = anterior scar causing extension loss
- PCL: May stretch rather than disrupt — look for enlarged, gray ligament on short TE
- Bone contusion pattern (posterolateral tibial plateau) → look for ACL tear — see [[bone-contusion]]
