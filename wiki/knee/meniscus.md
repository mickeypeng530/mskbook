# Meniscus

## Anatomy

### Structure
- C-shaped **fibrocartilaginous** structures; thick peripherally, thin centrally
- **Medial meniscus**: Posterior horn > anterior horn (always)
- **Lateral meniscus**: Anterior horn = posterior horn
- If posterior horn is smaller than anterior horn → suspect tear or prior partial meniscectomy

### Vascularity
- Greatest near periphery, almost nonexistent near free edge
- Peripheral tears → reparable; central tears → often unrepairable

### MRI Appearance
- **Normal**: Devoid of signal on all sequences
- **Children/young adults**: Some intermediate signal near posterior horn capsular attachment (normal vascularity — not degeneration)
- **Body segments**: 2-3 contiguous bow-tie images on sagittal (4 mm slices)
- **Anterior/posterior horns**: 3-4 sagittal images, triangular configuration

### Body Segment Ratio Rule
- Normal ratio of body segments to horn images: **1:2 or 1:3**
- Too many horn images (small body) → bucket-handle, flipped, or radial tear
- Too many body images → discoid meniscus

## Pathology: Meniscal Tears

### MRI Diagnosis
- **Tear** = High signal that **disrupts the articular surface**
- Signal that does NOT reach articular surface = **intrasubstance/myxoid degeneration** (not a tear, not symptomatic, not treated)
- ~90% of cases are definitive; ~10% are borderline — describe findings and let clinical exam guide
- **Sensitivity decreases with ACL tear** — look closely for peripheral and posterior horn lateral meniscus tears

### Types of Meniscal Tears

| Type | Description | Key MRI Finding |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Oblique/horizontal** | Most common; undersurface of posterior horn medial meniscus; degenerative | Signal to undersurface |
| **Vertical longitudinal** | May progress to bucket-handle | Vertical signal on sagittal/coronal |
| **Bucket-handle** | ~10% of tears; inner fragment displaced into notch | Loss of bow tie; double PCL sign |
| **Radial (free edge)** | Perpendicular to long axis; disrupts hoop strength | Ghost, cleft, or truncated triangle |
| **Flap** | Fragment can flip medially or inferiorly | Absent/thin body segment |
| **Medially flipped flap** | Flap flips into medial gutter | Thinner body + fragment below meniscus on coronal |
| **Peripheral** | At meniscocapsular junction; ramp lesions with ACL tears | Subtle; fluid-sensitive sequences |
| **Root tear** | At posterior root attachment | Gap at attachment; meniscal extrusion |
| **Meniscocapsular separation** | Fluid between MCL and meniscus | Fluid on coronal; mimics MCL sprain clinically |

### Bucket-Handle Tear Signs
- **Absent bow tie** — only 1 body segment instead of 2-3
- **Double PCL sign** — displaced fragment beneath PCL on sagittal
- Displaced fragment in intercondylar notch on coronal

### Radial Tear Appearances
1. **Ghost meniscus** — slice parallel to full-thickness radial tear; meniscus appears absent
2. **Cleft** — most reliable sign; slice perpendicular to tear
3. **Truncated triangle** — slice parallel to tear

### Root Tear
- Radial tear at posterior root attachment
- → Loss of hoop strength → **meniscal extrusion** off tibia
- → Accelerated OA in affected compartment
- Look for gap at root attachment on coronal/sagittal

### What to Report
1. Type of tear
2. Location (anterior horn, posterior horn, body)
3. Which surface(s) involved
4. Length of tear
5. Displaced fragments/flaps
6. Associated meniscal cyst
7. Associated meniscal extrusion

## Pathology: Meniscal Cysts

### Parameniscal Cysts
- Associated with **horizontal tear** (ball-valve mechanism)
- Fluid extrudes through tear and becomes trapped at meniscocapsular junction
- Alert surgeon to underlying tear — cyst recurs if tear not addressed
- Parameniscal component usually shows T2 fluid signal

### Intrameniscal Cysts
- Usually do NOT show T2 fluid signal — resemble intrasubstance degeneration
- May cause mass effect / swelling of meniscus
- Collapsed intrameniscal cyst may mimic horizontal tear

## Discoid Meniscus {#discoid}

- Congenital malformation — wider than normal, may be disk-shaped
- Diagnosed when **> 2 body segments** (bow ties) on sagittal
- **Lateral** meniscus most commonly affected (~3%); medial is rare
- More prone to cystic degeneration and tears
- Can be symptomatic even without tear

### Wrisberg Variant
- Discoid lateral meniscus lacking normal capsular attachments (struts/fascicles)
- Only attachment at posterior horn = meniscofemoral ligament of **Wrisberg**
- Posterior horn can sublux/fold into joint with knee flexion
- Typically seen in **children**
- Treatment: Reattachment to capsule (NOT meniscectomy — premature OA)
- Look for absent normal struts around popliteus tendon

## Pitfalls

### Posterior Horn Lateral Meniscus (4 pitfalls)
1. **Meniscofemoral ligament insertion** (Humphrey or Wrisberg) — present in ~75%; mimics peripheral tear; follow ligament to PCL on sequential sagittal images
2. **Popliteal artery pulsation artifact** — extends through meniscus; fix by swapping phase/frequency direction
3. **Magic angle phenomenon** — ill-defined intermediate signal on short TE; disappears on T2
4. **Popliteus tendon** — passes between meniscus and capsule; mimics tear; do not confuse with vertical longitudinal tear (common with ACL tear)

### Anterior Horn Lateral Meniscus
- **Speckled appearance** from ACL fibers inserting into meniscus — seen in **60%** of normal patients

### Transverse Ligament
- Insertion on anterior horn mimics tear (especially laterally)
- Follow it across Hoffa's fat pad on sequential sagittal images

### Other Pitfalls for Absent Bow Tie
- Children or small adults (normally fewer body segments)
- Postoperative meniscus
- Severe OA or elderly (>65) — free edge worn away

---

## Key Points
- Short TE is mandatory for meniscal evaluation — long TE misses tears
- Tear = signal disrupting articular surface; degeneration = signal not reaching surface
- Count bow ties: <2 → tear/cyst; >2 → discoid
- Double PCL sign = bucket-handle tear
- Ghost meniscus, cleft, truncated triangle = radial tear
- ACL tear → closely inspect posterior horn lateral meniscus and peripheral tears
- Meniscal extrusion → look for root tear
- Meniscocapsular separation: Immobilize or repair (NOT brace and play)
