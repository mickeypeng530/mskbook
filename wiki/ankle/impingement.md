# Ankle Impingement & Sinus Tarsi

## Anterolateral Impingement Syndrome {#anterolateral}

### Anatomy of Anterolateral Gutter
- Boundaries: AITFL + ATFL (anterior), talus (medial), fibula (lateral), tibial plafond/syndesmosis (superior), CFL (inferior)

### Etiology
- Post-traumatic (inversion injury) → hypertrophic synovium, fibrotic scar, or accessory fascicle of AITFL

### Clinical
- Anterolateral ankle pain, swelling, limited dorsiflexion
- Clinically indistinguishable from other causes of anterolateral pain

### MRI
- **Low signal soft tissue** deep to AITFL and ATFL where none normally present
- Joint effusion aids recognition
- Pure fibrotic scar: NO T2 high signal (low on all sequences)

## Sinus Tarsi Syndrome {#sinus-tarsi}

### Anatomy
- Cone-shaped space between calcaneus and talus
- **Narrow end medial**, wide end lateral (beneath lateral malleolus)
- Contents: fat, ligaments (cervical + interosseous), neurovascular structures, joint capsule
- Function: **proprioception** for hindfoot

### Etiology
- **70%** trauma (inversion injury)
- **30%** inflammatory arthropathy
- **80%** have concomitant torn lateral ligament

### MRI
- Key finding: **obliteration of sinus tarsi fat**
- T1W: low signal replacing normal fat
- T2W: high signal (acute/inflammatory) OR low signal (chronic/fibrotic)
- **Partial** fat obliteration: unlikely to be sinus tarsi syndrome

> **⚠️ Pitfall**: Large joint/subtalar effusion extending into sinus tarsi ≠ abnormal sinus tarsi. Sinus tarsi syndrome is a **clinical diagnosis** — MRI abnormalities do NOT always correlate with symptoms. Describe findings, do not diagnose in report.

## Os Trigonum Syndrome (Posterior Impingement) {#os-trigonum}

### Mechanism
- Os trigonum (or trigonal process) + [[tendons#fhl]] trapped between posterior tibia and calcaneus during **forced plantar flexion**

### Demographics
- Ballet dancers, running downhill, kicking, basketball

### MRI Findings
- **Marrow edema** in trigonal process / os trigonum
- High T2W at synchondrosis (fracture of os trigonum–talus junction)
- Focal/loculated high signal fluid around FHL (**stenosing tenosynovitis**)
- May see loose bodies

### Association
- FHL stenosing tenosynovitis (see [[tendons#fhl]])
