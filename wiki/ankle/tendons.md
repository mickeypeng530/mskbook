# Ankle Tendons

## Achilles Tendon {#achilles}

### Normal MRI
- Low signal on ALL sequences; flat or concave anterior margin on axial
- Normal AP diameter: **~7 mm**; parallel anterior/posterior margins on true sagittal
- **Kager's fat triangle**: triangular fat pad anterior to tendon
- **No tendon sheath** — paratenon on dorsal, medial, lateral aspects
- Vertical line of high signal in substance = normal (gastrocnemius/soleus interface or small vessels)

### Achilles Tears
- Most common location: **3-4 cm above calcaneal insertion**; also at musculotendinous junction and distal end
- Demographics: middle-aged, unconditioned athletes
- **Partial tear**: fluid-bright T2 signal within tendon + thickening
- **Tendinosis vs partial tear**: tendinosis does NOT become fluid-bright on T2

### Xanthoma
- Stippled pattern: intermediate signal foci between low-signal collagen fibers
- **Bilateral**; from familial hyperlipidemia types II/III
- Indistinguishable from tendinosis/partial tear on MRI

### Haglund's Syndrome (Pump Bump) {#haglund}
Triad:
1. **Retrocalcaneal bursitis** — teardrop-shaped bursa between tendon and posterior superior calcaneus
2. **Achilles tendon bursitis** (retro-Achilles) — adventitious bursa posterior to distal Achilles
3. **Partial tearing** of distal Achilles

> **⚠️ Pitfall — Plantaris tendon**: Present in 90%; lies anteromedial to Achilles. High signal plane between plantaris and Achilles mimics partial Achilles tear. Completely torn Achilles: plantaris may be mistaken for remaining intact fibers. Resolution: trace on sequential axial images.

## Medial Ankle Tendons {#medial-tendons}

### Mnemonic: "Tom, Dick, And Harry" (anterior to posterior)
| Letter | Tendon | Key Feature |
|--------|--------|-------------|
| **Tom** | Tibialis posterior (PTT) | Largest; oval; ~2x size of FDL/FHL |
| **Dick** | Flexor digitorum longus (FDL) | Rarely abnormal |
| **And** | Posterior tibial artery, nerve, vein | — |
| **Harry** | Flexor hallucis longus (FHL) | Most lateral |

- Held by flexor retinaculum within tarsal tunnel; separate tendon sheaths

### Posterior Tibial Tendon (PTT) {#ptt}
- Pulley: medial malleolus
- Inserts primarily on medial navicular; slips to 3 cuneiforms and bases of 1st-4th metatarsals
- **Most commonly abnormal** medial tendon
- Demographics: middle-aged/older women, rheumatoid arthritis

#### PTT Pathology
- **Partial tear**: high T2 signal, thickening, or thinning/attenuation
- **Longitudinal split tear**: two PTTs on axial images — same clinical significance as complete tear
- **Functional consequence**: loss of arch support → **flatfoot deformity**
- Secondary signs: loss of longitudinal arch; periosteal reaction on posterior medial malleolus

> **⚠️ Pitfall**: Distal PTT near navicular insertion normally appears thickened with intermediate/high signal (multiple slips). Do NOT call partial tear.

#### Accessory Navicular (Os Naviculare) {#accessory-navicular}

| Type | Description | Clinical |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **I** | Small sesamoid within PTT | Asymptomatic |
| **II** | Large secondary ossification center (~10% population) | Painful separation with trauma; **Kidner procedure** |
| **Cornuate** | Large medial navicular tubercle | Higher incidence of PTT tears |

#### PTT-Associated Findings
- **[[ligaments#spring-ligament]]** abnormality (frequently co-occur)
- Sinus tarsi syndrome
- DJD of posterior subtalar joint

### Flexor Hallucis Longus (FHL) {#fhl}
- Course: groove on medial posterior talus → beneath sustentaculum tali (pulley) → between hallux sesamoids → distal phalanx of great toe
- **Sheath communicates with ankle joint in 20%** — fluid in sheath does NOT equal tenosynovitis if ankle effusion present

> **⚠️ Pitfall**: Normal to have more fluid in FHL sheath than other medial tendons. With ankle effusion, fluid in FHL sheath is NOT tenosynovitis.

#### FHL Pathology
- **Stenosing tenosynovitis**: focal, asymmetric fluid pooling; associated with **[[impingement#os-trigonum]]**
- Distal tenosynovitis/partial tears at hallux sesamoid level: runners, ballet dancers en pointe
- Tears at ankle level are rare

## Lateral Ankle Tendons (Peroneals) {#peroneals}

### Normal Anatomy
- Peroneus brevis + longus: posterolateral ankle; primary **everters**
- Pulley: lateral malleolus
- Common sheath proximally; separate sheaths distally
- **Brevis**: anterior to longus; flat/broad; muscle belly extends more distally
- **Longus**: more rounded; inserts plantar on base of 1st metatarsal + medial cuneiform
- Separated by **peroneal tubercle** on lateral calcaneus
- Brevis normal shape: flat or oval; **C-shaped (boomerang) = ABNORMAL**

### Peroneus Brevis Longitudinal Split Tear {#peroneal-split}
- Vertical tear at level of tip of lateral malleolus
- Mechanism: wedging of brevis between fibula and longus during dorsiflexion
- Peroneus longus also partially torn in **~30%**
- Reactive: sharp posterolateral fibular spur

#### Predisposing Factors
- Torn/lax superior peroneal retinaculum
- Flat or convex posterior lateral malleolus (normal = concave)
- Low-lying peroneus brevis muscle belly
- Accessory peroneus quartus muscle

> **⚠️ Pitfall**: Bifurcate brevis tendon or peroneus quartus may simulate split tear. **Split tear** = one muscle belly → two tendons; **Bifurcate** = separate muscle bellies; **Peroneus quartus** = separate muscle from brevis.

### Peroneal Tendon Dislocation
- Superior peroneal retinaculum disruption from forced inversion + plantar flexion
- MRI: tendons lateral to fibula instead of posterior
- Predisposing: shallow/hypoplastic retromalleolar groove

### Painful Os Peroneus Syndrome {#os-peroneus}
- Os peroneus (sesamoid in peroneus longus) in ~10%
- MRI: marrow edema in os peroneus near cuboid tunnel
- Can be diagnosed before tendon rupture

> **Association**: Lateral collateral ligament tears commonly co-exist with peroneus brevis splits

## Anterior Ankle Tendons {#anterior-tendons}

### Anatomy (medial to lateral)
1. **Anterior tibial** (largest, most medial)
2. Extensor hallucis longus
3. Extensor digitorum longus
4. Peroneus tertius

- Function: dorsiflexion; seldom pathological

### Anterior Tibial Tendon
- Most likely anterior tendon to be abnormal
- Tears: increasing age, running on hills

> **⚠️ Pitfall**: Tear often presents clinically as a **mass** (may be suspected as tumor). Imaging needed to assess tendon retraction.
