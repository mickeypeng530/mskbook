# Glenohumeral Ligaments

## Anatomy
The glenohumeral ligaments are **thickenings of the anterior joint capsule**. Together with the [[labrum]], they form the **glenohumeral labroligamentous complex**.

### Superior Glenohumeral Ligament (SGHL)
- **Origin**: Superior glenoid tubercle, anterior to biceps tendon origin
- **Insertion**: Superior aspect of lesser tuberosity, blending with coracohumeral ligament
- **Function**: Prevents **inferior displacement** of humerus during abduction
- **MRI**: Seen at the level of the **body of the coracoid process** on axial images; parallels the coracoid

### Middle Glenohumeral Ligament (MGHL)
- **Origin**: Anterosuperior labrum, below SGHL
- **Insertion**: Medial aspect of lesser tuberosity
- **Function**: Contributes to **anterior stability** with subscapularis tendon
- **MRI**: Seen at and just inferior to the **tip of the coracoid** on axial images; lies deep to subscapularis, adjacent to anterior labrum
- **Most variable** of the three — **absent in ~30%** of shoulders (absence does NOT increase instability)

### Inferior Glenohumeral Ligament (IGHL)
- **Origin**: Inferior two-thirds of anterior and posterior labrum
- **Insertion**: Surgical neck of the humerus
- **Components**: Anterior band + posterior band + intervening axillary pouch
- **Function**: **Most important stabilizer** of the glenohumeral joint
  - Limits anterior translation with abduction + external rotation
  - Limits posterior translation with internal rotation
- **MRI**: Seen on axial images through the inferior glenoid, attaching to anterior and posterior labrum

### En Face Configuration
- When viewed from the front (coronal), the three ligaments form a **Z configuration**

## MRI Appearance
- **Best planes**: Axial + sagittal oblique
- All three ligaments identifiable on sagittal oblique images
- MR arthrography provides best visualization by distending the joint

## Biomechanics of Injury
- Position of arm at time of injury determines which ligament is stressed
- **Abduction + external rotation** → tension on anterior band of IGHL → anterior labral/ligament injury → [[bankart-lesion]]
- ABER position (abduction and external rotation) tightens the inferior labroligamentous complex, making labral tears more conspicuous

## Common Pathology
- [[bankart-lesion]] — IGHL-labral complex avulsion
- HAGL lesion — humeral avulsion of glenohumeral ligament (usually IGHL)
- BHAGL lesion — bony humeral avulsion of glenohumeral ligament
- Thickened, torn, or absent ligaments → manifestation of instability

## Imaging Pitfalls
- **MGHL mimics anterior labral tear** on axial images — normal structure anterior to labrum
- MGHL absence (30%) is normal — do not report as pathologic
- Thick MGHL in [[labrum|Buford complex]] may be confused with displaced labral fragment
