# Rotator Interval

## Anatomy
- **Shape**: Triangular space between [[rotator-cuff|supraspinatus]] (superior) and [[rotator-cuff|subscapularis]] (inferior) tendons
- **Base**: Coracoid process
- **Apex**: Transverse humeral ligament (roof of bicipital groove)
- **Anterior border**: Capsule, superior glenohumeral ligament, coracohumeral ligament
- **Contents**: Long biceps tendon courses through the interval
- This is the site where arthroscopists enter the shoulder joint

## MRI Appearance
- **Best plane**: Sagittal oblique
- **T1W sagittal oblique**: Normal subcoracoid/retrocoracoid fat should be visible
- The interval is bordered by low signal tendons superiorly and inferiorly

## Common Pathology

### Rotator Interval Tears
- **Causes**: Anterior GH dislocations, glenohumeral instability, arthroscopy (surgical defect)
- **Best sequences**: Fluid-sensitive (T2W) or post-contrast T1W sagittal oblique
- **Findings**:
  - Patulous rotator interval
  - High signal through coracohumeral ligament and capsule
  - May communicate with subacromial/subdeltoid bursa → **mimics rotator cuff tear** on MR arthrography

### Adhesive Capsulitis
- Scar tissue forms around SGHL and coracohumeral ligament in the rotator interval
- **Key sign**: Obliteration of subcoracoid fat on T1W sagittal images
  - Highly specific but lacks sensitivity
- Thickening of anterior capsular structures
- Patients with [[adhesive-capsulitis]] typically have little or no joint fluid
- **Pitfall**: Large joint effusion can also replace subcoracoid fat (but easily recognized on T2W)

## Imaging Pitfalls
- Torn rotator interval communicating with subacromial bursa may be mistaken for a cuff tear
- Distinguish from true cuff tear by intact supraspinatus and subscapularis tendons
