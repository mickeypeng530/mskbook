# Parsonage-Turner Syndrome

## Definition
Acute brachial neuritis — probably viral inflammation of peripheral nerves around the shoulder. Exact etiology unknown. Also known as **neuralgic amyotrophy**.

## Pathophysiology
- Likely viral inflammatory process affecting brachial plexus nerves
- **Precipitating factors**:
  - Recent viral infection
  - Recent vaccination
  - General anesthesia
- Seen in about **1%** of shoulder MRI studies

## Clinical Presentation
- **Sudden onset** of severe shoulder pain
- **Delayed weakness** follows in 48-72 hours (no trauma history)
- Self-limited but prolonged course — may last **1 year or more**
- **Bilateral in 30%** of cases

## Components — Nerves and Muscles Affected

| Nerve | Muscles |
|-------|---------|
| Suprascapular | Supraspinatus, infraspinatus |
| Axillary | Teres minor, deltoid |
| Subscapular | Subscapularis |
| Long thoracic | Serratus anterior |

## MRI Findings

### Phases
| Phase | T1 Finding | T2 Finding |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Acute/subacute** (edema) | Normal | **High signal in affected muscles** (neurogenic edema) |
| **Chronic** (atrophy) | **High signal** (fatty infiltration) | Variable |

- Pattern of muscle involvement follows **nerve distribution**, not a single tendon
- Multiple muscles affected simultaneously — distinguishes from isolated nerve entrapment

## Differential Diagnosis
- [[suprascapular-nerve-entrapment]] — mass compressing nerve (MRI shows the mass)
- Quadrilateral space syndrome — teres minor ± deltoid only
- Brachial plexus injury — history of trauma
- [[rotator-cuff-tear]] — tendon discontinuity, not diffuse muscle edema

## Pitfalls
- MRI **cannot distinguish** Parsonage-Turner from brachial plexus/nerve injury by imaging alone — clinical presentation is key (no trauma, sudden pain → delayed weakness)
- Was not described in radiology literature until **fat-suppressed imaging** became routine (needed to see neurogenic edema)
- May be mistaken for rotator cuff pathology if muscle edema is not recognized as neurogenic

## Key Points
- Sudden pain without trauma → weakness 48-72 hours later = classic presentation
- MRI: Diffuse muscle edema (T2 high signal) in nerve distribution pattern
- Self-limited but prolonged (up to 1+ year); bilateral in 30%
- Clinical diagnosis supported by MRI — no mass, no tendon tear, nerve-distribution muscle edema
- Precipitating factors: viral infection, vaccination, general anesthesia
