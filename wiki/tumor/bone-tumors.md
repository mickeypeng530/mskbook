# Bone Tumors

## Approach to Interpretation

### Age-Location Guide
| Age | Consider First |
|-----|---------------|
| 10-25 years | Primary bone tumors |
| >40 years | **Metastases first** |

| Location | Typical Tumors |
|----------|---------------|
| **Epiphysis** | GCT, chondroblastoma |
| **Metaphysis** | Osteosarcoma, NOF, osteochondroma |
| **Diaphysis** | Ewing sarcoma, lymphoma |

### Aggressive-Appearing BENIGN Lesions (Pitfalls)
These benign lesions can display very aggressive MRI appearance due to associated edema:
- **Osteoid osteoma**: Cortical nidus + extensive surrounding edema
- **Chondroblastoma**: Epiphyseal + striking edema extending to metaphysis/soft tissues
- **Osteoblastoma**: Spine posterior elements; >2 cm, expansile
- **Eosinophilic granuloma (LCH)**: Variable location; beveled edge on XR
- **Stress fracture**: Linear fracture line within edema; **AVOID BIOPSY** — immature osteoid mimics malignancy at histology

## Bone Lesions with HIGH T1 Signal {#high-t1}

High T1 = FAT or specific tissue compositions

| Lesion | Location | T1 | T2 | Key Feature |
|--------|----------|----|----|-------------|
| **Intraosseous lipoma** | Calcaneus, proximal femur | HIGH (fat) | Inter-High | Parallels fat; may have central cystic/calcific change |
| **Hemangioma (spine)** | Vertebral body (common) | HIGH (fat) | HIGH | "Polka-dot" axial; aggressive type lacks fat (low T1) |
| **Bone infarct** | Distal femur, proximal tibia | HIGH (central fat) | Variable | **Serpentine low signal margin**; "double-line sign" on T2 |
| **Paget's disease** | Pelvis, spine, skull | Variable | Variable | Cortical thickening, bone enlargement; better on XR |

- **Lipoma vs Infarct**: Lipoma = homogeneous fat; Infarct = geographic + serpentine margin

---

## Bone Lesions with LOW T2 Signal {#low-t2}

Low T2 = sclerosis, calcification, fibrous tissue

| Lesion | Key Features |
|--------|-------------|
| **Bone island (enostosis)** | Extremely low T2; stable; no soft tissue component |
| **NOF / Fibrous cortical defect** | Adolescents; eccentric metaphyseal; low-intermediate T2; easily diagnosed on XR |
| **Fibrous dysplasia** | NO characteristic MRI appearance — heterogeneous; XR "ground glass" more diagnostic |
| **Primary lymphoma of bone** | Often low T2 (fibrosis); B-cell; variable appearance |

---

## Fluid-Fluid Levels {#fluid-levels}

Stagnant blood products within cavernous spaces

### Differential Diagnosis

| Lesion | Key Distinguishing Feature |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **ABC (primary)** | Non-enhancing spaces fill **ENTIRE** lesion; expansile, lobular |
| **Telangiectatic osteosarcoma** | Associated **solid mass** component |
| **GCT + secondary ABC** | Solid enhancing component present |
| **Chondroblastoma** | **Epiphyseal**, skeletally immature |
| **Fibrous dysplasia** | Ground-glass matrix on XR |
| **MFH of bone** | Aggressive features, older patient |

### Rule of Thirds
- **>2/3** of lesion filled with fluid-fluid levels = usually **BENIGN** (often ABC)
- **<1/3** = consider **MALIGNANCY**
- Associated **solid mass** → biopsy required (secondary ABC or malignancy)

## Cartilaginous Tumors {#cartilage}

### Enchondroma
- Multiple lobules of hyaline cartilage
- T2: Homogeneously **HIGH** within lobules
- Internal septa: Thin, low signal (fibrous)
- Low signal foci: Calcified matrix
- Enhancement: **"Rings and arcs"** pattern (vessels in septa; cartilage does NOT enhance)
- **NO surrounding edema** (key benign feature)

### Enchondroma vs Chondrosarcoma

| Feature | Favors Chondrosarcoma |
|---------|-----------------------|
| Deep endosteal scalloping | >2/3 cortex |
| Cortical destruction ± soft tissue mass | Highly concerning |
| Adjacent edema on STIR | Worrisome |
| Size >5 cm | More likely malignant |
| Axial skeleton location | Higher risk than appendicular |

- **LIMITATION**: Often DIFFICULT or IMPOSSIBLE to distinguish enchondroma from low-grade chondrosarcoma — same MRI appearance possible

### Osteochondroma {#osteochondroma}
- Most common benign bone tumor; 70-80% diagnosed <20 years
- MRI: Cortex and medullary cavity **continuous with parent bone**
- T2: HIGH cartilage cap clearly visible
- **Cap <1 cm** = benign; **Cap >2 cm** = **SUSPICIOUS for malignant degeneration** (chondrosarcoma)
- Complications: NV impingement, bursal formation, fracture

### Chondroblastoma
- Rare **EPIPHYSEAL** tumor; age 5-25 years
- Low T1, heterogeneous T2 (chondroid matrix + fibrous stroma + calcifications)
- With aggressive features (edema, cortical expansion) → Enneking 3

## Specific Malignant Bone Tumors

### Osteosarcoma {#osteosarcoma}
- 20% of primary malignant bone tumors; peak age **10-15 years**
- Location: **Metaphysis** — distal femur > proximal tibia > humerus
- Subtypes: Osteoblastic (50%), chondroblastic (25%), fibroblastic (25%)
- Variants: Parosteal (low-grade, juxta-articular), periosteal (crater-like cortical erosion), telangiectatic (blood-filled, mimics ABC)
- MRI: Low signal rim (periosteal reaction); dense sclerotic components = low on all
- **SKIP LESIONS IN 25%** — always use body coil survey

### Ewing Sarcoma {#ewing}
- 10% of primary bone tumors; highly malignant; primarily **children** (peak 5-15 years)
- Location: Lower skeleton — pelvis, sacrum, femur, tibia; **metadiaphyseal/diaphyseal**
- Clinical: Fever, local inflammation → **may mimic osteomyelitis**
- MRI: Low-signal periosteal thickening/lamination ("onion-skin")
- Treatment: Radiation + chemotherapy → resection

### Chondrosarcoma
- Third most common (~20%); usually **>45 years**; ~45% in pelvis
- MRI: Low-signal calcifications; **scalloped contour on T2** (lobulated high-signal cartilage)
- **Ring-and-arc enhancement** on T1+Gd — highly specific for low-grade
- Variants: Undifferentiated (high-grade), clear cell (low-grade, resembles chondroblastoma), juxta-articular

### Giant Cell Tumor {#gct}
- Location: **EPIPHYSIS** of long bones (distal femur, proximal tibia); also sacrum
- Age: 20-40 years; extends from epiphysis to metaphysis, abuts articular surface
- T1: Low, sharply outlined; T2: Heterogeneous
- **Hemosiderin** deposits common (low signal both T1/T2)
- Tends to recur — detecting recurrence is major MRI role

## Other Benign Bone Tumors

### Osteoid Osteoma
- Osteoid-producing lesion **≤1.5 cm**; age 10-35; night pain relieved by aspirin
- Types: Cortical (80-90%), medullary, subperiosteal, intra-articular
- MRI: Nidus (low T1, high T2 if not calcified) + extensive edema
- **CT is modality of choice** for imaging nidus

### Osteoblastoma
- Related to osteoid osteoma but **>1.5 cm**; vertebral bodies predilection
- Pseudomalignant variant = Enneking 3

### ABC (Aneurysmal Bone Cyst)
- 80% in patients <20 years; metaphyses + posterior vertebral elements
- Rim of low signal + multiple central areas with variable signal
- **Fluid-fluid levels** in well-defined cystic cavities (classic)
- Treatment: Curettage; recurrence → radical excision or embolization

### Unicameral Bone Cyst
- Children 3-14 years; originates near epiphysis, migrates to diaphysis
- Smooth low-signal rim; fluid-filled (low T1, high T2); contrast NOT necessary

### NOF (Nonossifying Fibroma)
- Developmental; metaphysis of distal femur/tibia; childhood/adolescence
- Early: Slightly increased T1, low T2 (fibrous + hemosiderin)
- Healed: Low signal/signal void on all sequences

### Fibrous Dysplasia
- Disordered bone maturation; monostotic vs polyostotic (Albright syndrome)
- Location: Proximal femur, tibia, mandible, ribs
- MRI: Homogeneous low T1; majority high T2 (some equal to muscle)
- No characteristic MRI appearance — **XR (ground glass) more diagnostic**

### Eosinophilic Granuloma (LCH)
- Children/adolescents/young adults; skull, mandible, spine, ribs, long bones
- Initial: Aggressive destruction, edema; Late: Sharply outlined, little edema
- Characteristic: Vertebra plana, beveled skull lesion, central sequestra
- DDx (early): Osteomyelitis, Ewing sarcoma

## Metastases {#metastases}

- **MAJORITY** of malignancies in bones
- Signal: Low T1, high T2, enhancement; most sensitive: STIR, contrast-enhanced
- Exceptions: Hemorrhagic (high T1), melanotic (high T1), osteoblastic (low on ALL sequences)
- Morphology: Round/well-circumscribed early → ill-defined later
- Favors metastases: Advanced age, multiple lesions, known malignancy, red marrow sites

### DDx Pitfalls
- **Bone island vs blastic metastases**: Bone island = low on all, stable, no soft tissue
- **Red marrow reconversion vs infiltration**: Red marrow = symmetric, T2 not markedly high, no focal mass
- **Schmorl's nodes vs vertebral metastases**: Schmorl's = endplate indentation, surrounding sclerosis, stable

---

## Key Points
- XR first — most specific; MRI for staging; CT for matrix/cortex
- Skip lesions in 25% of osteosarcomas — body coil survey mandatory
- Fluid-fluid levels: >2/3 = likely benign; solid component → biopsy
- Osteochondroma cap >2 cm → suspect malignant degeneration
- Enchondroma vs low-grade chondrosarcoma often indistinguishable on MRI
- Stress fracture: AVOID BIOPSY (immature osteoid mimics malignancy)
- Age >40 + multiple bone lesions = metastases until proven otherwise
