Chapter 12: Bone and Soft-Tissue Tumors

Complete MRI Reference

PART I: GENERAL PRINCIPLES

▼
1 MRI ADVANTAGES ▼

MRI is superior to CT for musculoskeletal tumor evaluation

  • Superior soft-tissue contrast
  • Direct multiplanar imaging
  • Tissue characterization via signal patterns
  • Better assessment of neurovascular bundle infiltration

MRI vs CT Comparison:

  • MRI SUPERIOR: Intramedullary extent, soft tissue extension, neurovascular bundle, skip lesions, epiphyseal involvement
  • CT SUPERIOR: Cortical destruction, periosteal reaction, calcification, sclerotic changes
  • EQUAL: Joint involvement (both >90% specificity)

Key Statistics:

  • Intramedullary extent correlation with pathology: MRI r=0.99 vs CT r=0.86
  • Average difference from macroslides: MRI 4.9mm vs CT 16.5mm
  • Neurovascular bundle: MRI sensitivity 100%, specificity 98% vs CT 33%, 93%
2 COIL SELECTION ▼

First sequence: Body coil for large FOV to assess extent and detect skip lesions Then: Surface coil, torso phased-array, or circumferential coil for high spatial resolution Spine metastasis: Phased-array spinal coil

3 STANDARD PROTOCOL (UCSF) ▼

Sequence 1: T1 SE axial, TR 600, TE min, 5mm thickness — anatomy, marrow Sequence 2: T2 FSE + fat-sat axial, TR 3500, TE 60, 5mm — tumor extent, edema Sequence 3: T1 SE cor/sag, TR 600, TE min, 4mm — longitudinal extent Sequence 4: STIR/FMPIR cor/sag, TR 3500, TE 55, 4mm — marrow involvement Sequence 5: T1 SE + Gd + fat-sat axial, TR 600, TE min, 5mm — viable tumor, necrosis Sequence 6: T1 SE + Gd cor/sag — overall enhancement

4 SIGNAL CHARACTERISTICS ▼

Normal Tissue Signals:

  • Yellow bone marrow: High T1, Inter-High T2, Low STIR
  • Cortical bone: Low on all sequences
  • Muscle: Low T1, Intermediate T2
  • Subcutaneous fat: High T1, Inter-High T2, Low STIR

Tumor Signals:

  • Generic tumor: Low-Intermediate T1, High T2, High STIR
  • Fat-containing tumor: High T1, Inter-High T2, Low STIR
  • Cystic/Necrotic area: Low T1, High T2, No enhancement
  • Hemorrhage acute: Low-Inter T1, Low T2
  • Hemorrhage subacute (methemoglobin): High T1, High T2
  • Hemorrhage chronic (hemosiderin): Low T1, Low T2
  • Fibrosis/Scar: Low T1, Low T2

Tumors with HIGH T1 Signal (Short T1 Relaxation):

Fat-containing: Lipoma, Liposarcoma (well-differentiated), Vertebral hemangioma Methemoglobin-containing: Telangiectatic osteosarcoma, Hemorrhagic metastases, Post-chemo/radiation tumors, Melanotic melanoma metastases

Tumors with Fluid-Fluid Levels:

Aneurysmal bone cyst (most characteristic), Giant cell tumor, Chondroblastoma, Osteoblastoma, Telangiectatic osteosarcoma, Metastases, Abscess
5 BENIGN VS MALIGNANT DIFFERENTIATION ▼

Favors BENIGN:

  • Sharp, smooth, well-defined low-signal margin
  • Homogeneous signal on T2W
  • Minimal or absent peritumoral edema
  • Dynamic enhancement slope <30% (in 72% of cases)

Favors MALIGNANT:

  • Indistinct, irregular, infiltrative margin
  • Heterogeneous signal on T2W (necrosis, hemorrhage)
  • Extensive peritumoral edema
  • Dynamic enhancement slope >30% (in 84% of cases)
IMPORTANT: T1 and T2 relaxation time measurements are UNRELIABLE for distinguishing benign from malignant. Biopsy remains essential.
6 ENNEKING STAGING SYSTEM ▼

Based on three criteria

  • G (Grade): G0=benign, G1=low-grade malignant, G2=high-grade malignant
  • T (Local extent): T0=intracapsular, T1=extracapsular/intracompartmental, T2=extracompartmental
  • M (Metastasis): M0=no metastases, M1=metastases

Benign Stages:

  • Stage 1 (Latent): G0, T0, M0 — inactive, encapsulated
  • Stage 2 (Active): G0, T0, M0 — progressive growth, intracapsular
  • Stage 3 (Aggressive): G0, T1-T2, M0 — may penetrate capsule/cortex

Malignant Stages:

  • Stage IA: G1 (low-grade), T1 (intracompartmental), M0
  • Stage IB: G1, T2 (extracompartmental), M0
  • Stage IIA: G2 (high-grade), T1, M0
  • Stage IIB: G2, T2, M0
  • Stage III: Any G, Any T, M1 (metastases)
NOT APPLICABLE TO: Leukemias, Myelomas, Ewing sarcoma (for staging), Metastases
7 LOCAL STAGING ASSESSMENT ▼

Intramedullary Involvement:

  • MRI clearly superior to CT
  • Skip lesions occur in 25% of osteosarcomas
  • Body coil survey essential for detection

Epiphyseal Involvement:

  • MRI more sensitive than radiography
  • Study: 12/12 detected by MRI vs 9/12 by conventional XR

Joint Involvement:

  • CT and MRI equally accurate (specificity >90%)
  • Joint effusion is NOT a reliable sign of involvement
  • Absence of effusion is a good negative predictor

Cortical Destruction and Periosteal Reaction:

  • Conventional radiography and CT superior to MRI
  • GRE sequences increase MRI sensitivity for sclerotic changes

Soft-tissue Extension:

  • MRI clearly superior to enhanced CT
  • Greater tissue contrast between normal and abnormal

Neurovascular Bundle:

  • MRI superior (sens 100%, spec 98%, acc 98%)
  • CT inferior (sens 33%, spec 93%, acc 82%)
  • MR angiography excellent for vessel displacement vs encasement
8 PERITUMORAL EDEMA VS EXTRAOSSEOUS TUMOR ▼
Both show high signal on T1 post-contrast. Dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI can differentiate:
  • Viable tumor initial slope values >20% higher than edema
  • Use sequential images at 3.5-second intervals
  • Exponential-fitting algorithm for pixel-by-pixel parametric slope image
9 VIABLE VS NECROTIC TUMOR ▼

Viable tumor: Strong enhancement after contrast, high signal on post-Gd T1 Necrotic area: No enhancement, remains low-to-intermediate signal Dynamic contrast-enhanced MRI helps select optimal biopsy site

10 CHEMOTHERAPY RESPONSE MONITORING ▼

Morphologic Signs of Response:

  • Decrease in tumor volume
  • Improved delineation of muscle and intermuscular fatty septa
  • Appearance of cystic areas in tumor matrix
  • Development of dark rim (collagenous fibers)

Post-Chemotherapy MRI Patterns (Pan et al.):

1. DARK PATTERN: Low T1 and T2 → Good response, small residual foci 2. MOTTLED/SPECKLED: Inter T1, scattered high/low T2 → Low residual viable tumor 3. HOMOGENEOUS: Inter T1, high T2, no mottling → Macroscopic residual viable tumor 4. CYSTIC: Inter T1, multicystic/bubbly high T2 → High proportion peripheral viable tumor

Dynamic MRI Response Assessment:

  • Drop in enhancement slope ≥60% = response
  • Drop <60% = non-responder
11 RECURRENCE VS POST-TREATMENT CHANGES ▼

Causes of High T2 Signal After Treatment:

  • Tumor recurrence
  • Residual perineoplastic edema
  • Postsurgical changes (edema, hematoma, seroma, granulation tissue) — up to 6 months
  • Postradiation inflammatory changes
  • Fat necrosis, denervation atrophy

Suspicious for Recurrence:

  • Nodular lesions with low T1, high T2
  • Mass effect
  • Infiltrative extension
  • Osseous destruction

MTC (Magnetization Transfer Contrast):

  • Scar tissue: Strong MTC effect (>50%)
  • Tumor: Weak MTC effect (<25%)
  • Useful for differentiation

PART II: SPECIFIC TUMORS — MALIGNANT BONE TUMORS

▼
OSTEOSARCOMA ▼

Epidemiology: 20% of primary malignant bone tumors; peak age 10-15 years Location: Metaphysis of long tubular bones — distal femur > proximal tibia > humerus Histologic subtypes: Osteoblastic (50%), Chondroblastic (25%), Fibroblastic (25%)

Variants

  • Parosteal: Lower grade, juxta-articular, grows between cortex and muscle
  • Periosteal: Crater-like cortical erosion, chondroid matrix
  • Telangiectatic: Blood-filled pseudocysts, resembles ABC

MRI Findings

  • Low signal intensity rim on T1W and T2W (corresponds to periosteal reaction)
  • Dense sclerotic components: Low signal on all sequences
  • Cortical destruction and perineoplastic edema common
  • SKIP LESIONS IN 25% OF CASES — always use body coil survey
  • Cystic/necrotic changes and fluid levels frequent after chemotherapy
EWING SARCOMA ▼

Epidemiology: 10% of primary bone tumors; highly malignant; primarily children Age: 90% between 5-30 years; peak 5-15 years Location: Lower skeleton — 2/3 in pelvis, sacrum, femur, tibia; metadiaphyseal or diaphyseal Clinical: Fever, local inflammation — may mimic osteomyelitis

MRI Findings

  • Low-signal thickening and lamination of periosteum ("onion-skin" pattern)
  • Sclerotic areas in intramedullary component
  • Role of MRI: Staging and monitoring therapy response
Treatment: Radiation + chemotherapy, followed by resection
CHONDROSARCOMA ▼

Epidemiology: Third most common primary malignant bone tumor (~20%); usually >45 years Location: Long tubular bones; ~45% in pelvis

Variants

  • Undifferentiated: High-grade, aggressive, few calcifications
  • Clear cell: Low-grade, resembles chondroblastoma
  • Juxta-articular: Low-grade, arises from bone surface, late metastatic dissemination

MRI Findings

  • Low-signal calcifications in cartilaginous matrix
  • SCALLOPED CONTOUR ON T2W (lobulated high-signal cartilage)
  • RING-AND-ARC ENHANCEMENT on T1W+Gd — highly specific for low-grade chondrosarcoma
FIBROSARCOMA AND MALIGNANT FIBROUS HISTIOCYTOMA (BONE) ▼

Age: Even distribution 20-70 years Location: Ends of long tubular bones MRI: No characteristic findings; delineates extent; monitors chemo response

PRIMARY LYMPHOMA OF BONE ▼

Rare; metaphysis and epiphysis of long bones MRI: Heterogeneous T2 signal; low-signal areas = intratumoral fibrosis

ANGIOSARCOMA ▼

Similar to hemangioendothelioma and hemangiopericytoma MRI: Nonspecific; presence of vascular structures supports diagnosis

PART II: SPECIFIC TUMORS — BENIGN BONE TUMORS

▼
GIANT CELL TUMOR ▼

Location: EPIPHYSIS of long tubular bones (distal femur, proximal tibia); also sacrum Age: 20-40 years; predilection for males Characteristic: Arises in epiphysis and extends to metaphysis; abuts articular surface

MRI Findings

  • T1W: Low signal, sharply outlined
  • T2W: Heterogeneous — areas of decreased and increased signal
  • Hemosiderin deposits common — low signal on both T1 and T2
  • Tends to recur; detecting recurrence is major MRI role

Cardinal findings of local recurrence

  • Resorption of autogenous bone packed in curettaged lesion
  • Progressive osteolytic destruction
FIBROUS DYSPLASIA ▼

Disordered bone maturation with immature fibrous tissue Forms: Monostotic vs Polyostotic (Albright syndrome: café-au-lait spots, sexual precocity) Location: Proximal femur, proximal tibia, mandible, ribs; metaphysis and diaphysis

MRI Findings

  • Homogeneous low T1
  • Majority: Homogeneous high T2; some equal to muscle on T2 (~1/3)
  • Sclerotic rim and coarse trabeculations = signal void
NONOSSIFYING FIBROMA (FIBROXANTHOMA) ▼

Developmental periosteal aberration Location: Metaphysis of distal femur or distal tibia Age: Childhood/adolescence; heals after skeletal maturity

MRI Findings

  • Early: Slightly increased T1 (lipid-laden xanthomatous cells), low T2 (hemosiderin, fibrous tissue)
  • Healed: Low signal/signal void on all sequences
DESMOPLASTIC FIBROMA (DESMOID OF BONE) ▼

Location: Long tubular bones of young adults Behavior: Aggressive (Enneking 3); large sharply outlined trabeculated osteolytic lesions MRI: Nonspecific — low T1, high T2

INTRAOSSEOUS LIPOMA ▼

Location: Calcaneus and femoral neck MRI: High T1, intermediate-to-high T2 (fat signal); may have fat necrosis with calcifications

EOSINOPHILIC GRANULOMA (LANGERHANS CELL HISTIOCYTOSIS) ▼

Histiocytic infiltration of bone marrow Age: Children, adolescents, young adults; males predominant Location: Skull, mandible, spine, ribs, long tubular bones

Phases

  • Initial: Aggressive destruction, soft tissue component, periosteal reaction, extensive edema
  • Late: Sharply outlined lesion, little edema, possible sequester

MRI Findings

  • Decreased to slightly increased T1
  • Strongly increased T2
  • Strong enhancement
Characteristic manifestations: Vertebra plana, beveled lesion in skull, central sequestra

Differential diagnosis (early stage): Osteomyelitis, Ewing sarcoma

CHONDROMA (ENCHONDROMA) ▼

Common benign bone tumor Location: Metaphysis of long bones; hands/feet most common Composition: Mature hyaline cartilage with lacunae

Signs of Malignant Transformation

  • New pain
  • Increased bone scan uptake
  • Increasing heterogeneity
  • Cortical erosions
  • Periosteal reaction

MRI Findings

  • T1W: Slightly heterogeneous, predominantly low-signal lobulated lesion
  • T2W: Heterogeneous, predominantly high-signal lobulated lesion
  • Post-contrast: RING-AND-ARC enhancement pattern
  • Punctate and circular signal voids = calcifications of chondroid matrix
CHONDROBLASTOMA ▼

Rare EPIPHYSEAL tumor Location: Femur, humerus, tibia; foot (talus/calcaneus) = 10% Age: 5-25 years; usually painful Size: 90% ≤5-6 cm; well-defined, round, lobulated

MRI Findings

  • Low T1, heterogeneous T2
  • Heterogeneity with low-signal areas = chondroid matrix with fibrous stroma and calcifications
  • With aggressive features (marrow edema, cortical expansion, periosteal reaction) → Enneking 3
CHONDROMYXOID FIBROMA ▼

Uncommon; adolescents; asymptomatic Location: Eccentric lesion in metaphysis of long tubular bones Matrix: Chondroid, fibrous, and myxoid tissue; calcifications generally absent MRI: Decreased T1, increased T2; not diagnostic

OSTEOCHONDROMA (EXOSTOSIS) ▼
Common benign tumor; developmental or acquired Age: 70-80% diagnosed <20 years Location: Metaphyses of long tubular bones, pelvis, scapula

MRI Findings

  • Cortex and medullary cavity CONTINUOUS with parent bone
  • T1 cap: Intermediate intensity
  • T2 cap: Homogeneous high intensity (hyaline cartilage)
  • CARTILAGE CAP <1 cm = benign
  • CARTILAGE CAP >2 cm = consider malignant transformation to chondrosarcoma
Multiple osteochondromas (hereditary cartilaginous exostoses): Higher risk of malignant transformation
ANEURYSMAL BONE CYST ▼
Age: 80% in patients <20 years Location: Metaphyses of long bones and posterior vertebral elements Composition: Blood-filled spaces lined by granulation tissue, osteoid, multinucleated giant cells

MRI Findings

  • Rim of low signal intensity
  • Multiple central areas with variable signal intensities
  • FLUID-FLUID LEVELS in well-defined cystic cavities (classic finding)
  • Layering = intracystic hemorrhage of different ages
  • Intense enhancement after IV contrast
Treatment: Curettage; recurrences treated with radical excision or embolization
UNICAMERAL BONE CYST (SIMPLE BONE CYST) ▼

Cystic cavity with yellow transparent fluid Age: Children 3-14 years Location: Long bones; originates next to epiphysis, migrates to diaphysis

MRI Findings

  • Smooth low-signal or signal-void rim
  • Fluid-filled cavity: Low T1, high T2
  • Contrast enhancement NOT necessary for diagnosis
HEMANGIOMA (BONE) ▼

Incidental finding; middle-aged patients Location: Vertebral bodies, skull, facial bones

MRI Findings

  • Increased T1 and T2 (fatty component)
  • Post-contrast enhancement
  • Phleboliths appear as signal void

Vertebral hemangiomas

  • Predominantly fatty stroma + high T1 = inactive form
  • Predominantly soft-tissue stroma + low T1 = aggressive form (potential spinal cord compression)
OSTEOID OSTEOMA ▼

Osteoid-producing lesion ≤1.5 cm Age: 10-35 years Location: Long tubular bones especially lower extremities Clinical: Severe night pain relieved by aspirin

Types by Location

1. CORTICAL (80-90%): Nidus in cortex, <1.5 cm radiolucency; reactive sclerosis = signal void 2. MEDULLARY: Nidus in spongiosa (femoral neck, carpal/tarsal bones); large calcification 3. SUBPERIOSTEAL: Extremely rare; no reactive sclerosis 4. INTRA-ARTICULAR: Near joint; synovial inflammatory response

MRI Findings

  • Nidus: Decreased T1, increased T2 (if not calcified); signal void if calcified
  • Reactive sclerosis: Signal void
  • Extensive marrow edema and soft tissue edema with enhancement
NOTE: CT is modality of choice for imaging nidus; MRI better for edema but less accurate for nidus detection
OSTEOBLASTOMA ▼

Closely related to osteoid osteoma but >1.5 cm Location: Vertebral bodies predilection Age: 10-30 years Pseudomalignant osteoblastoma = stage 3

MRI: Same features as osteoid osteoma but more aggressive appearance; may calcify entirely

OSTEOMA ▼

Mature densely sclerotic bone Location: Skull, facial bones, paranasal sinuses; bone island (enostosis) MRI: SIGNAL VOID on all sequences

PART II: SPECIFIC TUMORS — MALIGNANT SOFT-TISSUE TUMORS

▼
GENERAL FEATURES OF MALIGNANT SOFT-TISSUE TUMORS ▼
  • Indistinct margins
  • Infiltrate surrounding structures
  • Heterogeneous T2 signal
  • Variable T1 signal (generally lower than fat)
  • Increased T2 relative to muscle
  • Exception: MFH can have low T2 signal (fibrous component)
MALIGNANT FIBROUS HISTIOCYTOMA (MFH) ▼

Most common malignant soft-tissue tumor in adults Age: Average 50 years Recurrence rate: Up to 45% Variants: Histiocytic, fibromatous, xanthomatous cells

MRI Findings

  • Low-to-intermediate T1
  • Heterogeneous high T2
  • Relatively strong, heterogeneous enhancement
  • Intratumoral necroses common
  • If fibromatous cells predominate: Low T2 signal
LIPOSARCOMA ▼

Frequent malignant tumor in adults Age: 40-60 years Location: Extremities and retroperitoneum

Variants (grade inversely proportional to fat content): 1. WELL-DIFFERENTIATED: Low-grade, resembles lipoma, fibrous septa; little/no enhancement 2. MYXOID (40-50%): Low-grade, lipoblasts, myxoid ground substance; high T2, lobulated, septations, fluid levels 3. ROUND CELL: High-grade, hypercellular 4. PLEOMORPHIC: High-grade, bizarre pleomorphic cells; heterogeneous, intense enhancement

Key Point: >5 cm, non-fatty components, thick septa (>2mm) → consider liposarcoma over lipoma

High recurrence rate

SYNOVIAL SARCOMA ▼
Origin: Soft tissues (NOT synovial lining); <10% intra-articular Age: Young adults Location: Lower extremity

Features

  • Extensive calcifications along periphery in 1/3 of patients
  • High recurrence rate

MRI Findings

  • Decreased T1, increased T2
  • Nonspecific signal pattern
  • Lobulated, smoothly outlined
  • Septations, fluid levels due to hemorrhage
ANGIOSARCOMA ▼

Rare Often arises on basis of chronic lymphedema (Stewart-Treves Syndrome after mastectomy) MRI: No specific criteria established

PART II: SPECIFIC TUMORS — BENIGN SOFT-TISSUE TUMORS

▼
LIPOMA ▼

Most common soft-tissue tumor Location: Subcutaneous (superficial) most common; also intramuscular, intermuscular, intraosseous, periosteal

MRI Findings

  • HIGH T1, intermediate-to-high T2 (follows fat signal on ALL sequences)
  • SUPPRESSES on fat-sat/STIR
  • Homogeneous, encapsulated, may have thin septa
Lipomatosis: Not encapsulated; various locations (mediastinum, abdomen, pelvis, epidural) Atypical lipoma: Contains non-fatty areas; propensity to recur; may undergo sarcomatous transformation
INTRAMUSCULAR MYXOMA ▼

Benign mesenchymal lesion Age: 50-70 years Location: Thigh, shoulder, upper arm, buttocks

MRI Findings

  • Sharply marginated within muscle
  • Low-intermediate T1 (lower than muscle)
  • Strongly increased T2 (cyst-like signal)
  • HETEROGENEOUS ENHANCEMENT (unlike true cyst)
Association with fibrous dysplasia (multiple intramuscular myxomas) Differential: Neurinoma (similar MRI pattern)
DESMOID (EXTRA-ABDOMINAL AGGRESSIVE FIBROMATOSIS) ▼

Location: Muscles, fasciae, aponeurosis — thigh, upper arm, buttock Age: 15-40 years Behavior: Poorly demarcated, infiltrates neighboring structures, does NOT metastasize Recurrence: 25-68%

MRI Findings

  • Homogeneous low signal on T1 (equal to or lower than muscle)
  • T2: Cellular areas have high signal; fibrous fascicles appear as linear or nodular signal voids
  • MRI findings multifarious but if fibrous fascicles prominent, diagnosis can be suggested
Other forms: Palmar fibromatosis (Dupuytren), Plantar fibromatosis (Ledderhose), Penile fibromatosis (Peyronie)
VASCULAR TUMORS: HEMANGIOMA, HEMANGIOENDOTHELIOMA, LYMPHANGIOMA, ANGIOLIPOMA ▼

Hemangioma (soft tissue):

  • Location: Skin and subcutaneous most common; also deep/intramuscular
  • Children: Usually skeletal musculature with muscle atrophy
  • Most sharply demarcated; can incorporate entire extremity (angiomatosis)
  • MRI: High T1 and T2 (fatty stroma); serpiginous high-signal vessels (dilated); phleboliths = signal void; intermediate-to-intense enhancement

Hemangioendothelioma (Hemangiopericytoma):

  • Semi-malignant variant with high incidence of malignant transformation
  • Imaging similar to lymphangiomas

Angiolipoma:

  • Heterogeneous signal pattern on T1 and T2
  • Areas of increased signal (fat) and high signal (hemorrhage)
  • Structures resembling vessels commonly visualized
SCHWANNOMA AND NEUROFIBROMA ▼

Age: All ages; most common 20-50 years Schwannoma: Typically solitary Neurofibroma: May be solitary or multiple (neurofibromatosis/Recklinghausen disease) Plexiform neurofibroma (giant neurofibroma): Arises from large nerve trunks; wormlike mass

Location: Upper extremity schwannomas typically in flexor region along large nerves Behavior: Grow slowly; freely movable perpendicular to nerve axis, not along axis

MRI Findings

  • Intermediate signal intensity on T1
  • Very high signal intensity on T2, occasionally heterogeneously distributed
  • Heterogeneity: Hypocellular and hypercellular areas, necrotic and fibrotic areas
  • Low-signal capsule often delineated
  • Round or ovoid in outline
TARGET SIGN on T2: High signal peripherally (myxomatous tissue), low signal center (fibrocollagenous tissue)

Malignant transformation signs: Large size, indistinct outline, infiltration of surrounding structures, severe heterogeneity, rapid growth MRI cannot reliably distinguish benign from malignant nerve sheath tumor

PIGMENTED VILLONODULAR SYNOVITIS (PVNS) ▼

Chronic inflammatory tumor Location: Knee most common; any joint can be involved Clinical: Intermittent pain and swelling

MRI Findings

  • T1: Heterogeneous — intermediate or decreased signal relative to muscle
  • T2: Heterogeneous, LOW SIGNAL INTENSITY (hemosiderin deposits)
  • GRE: Blooming artifact from hemosiderin
  • Possible osseous erosions on radiographs
GIANT CELL TUMOR OF TENDON SHEATH ▼

Histologically identical to PVNS Location: Synovial sheath; preferentially flexor tendons of fingers Behavior: Nodular chronic inflammation Recurrence: 9-20% Malignant transformation: Can occur

MRI Findings

  • Low-signal intensity along involved tendon on T1
  • Heterogeneous on T2
  • Low-signal areas = fibrotic areas or hemosiderin deposits
  • High-signal areas = inflamed synovial tissue
MYOGENIC TUMORS ▼

Can arise from striated (skeletal) or smooth musculature

Smooth Muscle:

  • Benign: Leiomyoma (cutaneous and deep), Angiomyoma (vascular leiomyoma), Epithelial leiomyoma
  • Malignant: Leiomyosarcoma, Epithelial leiomyosarcoma

Striated Muscle:

  • Benign: Adult rhabdomyoma, Genital rhabdomyoma, Fetal rhabdomyoma
  • Malignant: Rhabdomyosarcoma (embryonal, alveolar, pleomorphic, mixed), Ectomesenchymoma
MRI: Signal pattern nonspecific — decreased T1, increased T2 Suspicious for malignancy: Typical malignant criteria (indistinct outline, heterogeneity, high growth rate, large size)

PART II: METASTASES

▼
MRI CHARACTERISTICS ▼

Signal

  • Low T1-weighted
  • High T2-weighted
  • Enhancement after contrast administration

Most Sensitive Sequences

  • Fat-suppressed STIR
  • Contrast-enhanced sequences (especially subtraction technique)
  • T1-weighted for metastases in hematopoietically inactive yellow marrow

Exceptions

  • Hemorrhagic metastases: May have high T1 (methemoglobin)
  • Melanotic metastases of melanoma: High T1 (melanin)
  • Predominantly osteoblastic metastases: Low signal on ALL sequences

Morphology

  • Round, relatively well-circumscribed (early)
  • Progress to ill-defined lesions with increasing edema (later)
  • Vertebral metastases: Diffuse pathologic signal pattern throughout vertebral body
  • Pathologic fractures: Well delineated by radiography/CT, also visualized on MRI
DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS ▼

Factors Favoring Metastases

  • Advanced patient age
  • Multiple osseous lesions
  • Known malignancy
  • Rapid increase in size and number within short interval
  • Preferential location in hematopoietically active red marrow sites (increased perfusion)
Schmorl's Nodes vs Metastases:
  • Schmorl's nodes: Endplate indentations, surrounding sclerosis, absent or faint enhancement, stable appearance on follow-up

Vertebral Hemangiomas

  • Fat-equivalent signal pattern on T1 and T2
  • Only occasionally can distinguish from melanotic metastases of melanoma

Bone Cysts

  • Brighter than metastases on T1
  • Do not enhance
SUMMARY ▼

MRI is very sensitive for detecting metastases. Should be employed whenever conventional radiography or bone scans are suspicious, or do not adequately explain clinical findings (pain, elevated alkaline phosphatase). Early and purely osteolytic metastatic lesions may escape radiographic and scintigraphic detection but are detectable by MRI.

Cutaneous and other soft tissue metastases are rare. MRI is superior to radiography, sonography, and CT for soft-tissue evaluation. MRI indicated for search of suspected soft-tissue metastases when visible or palpable changes are absent.

APPENDIX: DIAGNOSTIC PITFALLS AND CLINICAL PEARLS

COMMON DIAGNOSTIC PITFALLS ▼

1. Bone island vs blastic metastases: - Bone island: All-sequence low signal, well-defined, NO soft tissue component, stable

2. Red marrow reconversion vs infiltration: - Red marrow: Symmetric distribution, T2 NOT markedly high, no focal mass

3. Osteochondroma malignant transformation: - Watch for: Cartilage cap >2cm, irregular thickening, new pain

4. GCT vs ABC: - GCT: More solid, epiphyseal location - ABC: Fluid-fluid levels more common, metaphyseal/spine

5. Lipoma vs Well-differentiated liposarcoma: - Suspect malignancy if: >5cm, non-fat components present, thick septa (>2mm)

CLINICAL PEARLS ▼

1. Age is the most important differentiating factor: - 10-25 years: Consider primary bone tumors - >40 years: Consider metastases first

2. Location guides diagnosis: - Epiphysis: GCT, Chondroblastoma - Metaphysis: Osteosarcoma, NOF, Osteochondroma - Diaphysis: Ewing sarcoma, Lymphoma

3. Signal pattern guidance: - High T1: Think fat or hemorrhage - All-sequence low: Think fibrous tissue or sclerosis

4. Reporting essentials: - Location (epi/meta/diaphysis, compartment) - Size (3D measurements) - Margin (sharp vs infiltrative) - Intramedullary extent (cm from joint line) - Skip lesions (body coil survey) - Cortex status - Soft tissue extension - Neurovascular bundle relationship (distance in mm) - Joint involvement - Enneking stage - Limb salvage feasibility - Biopsy site recommendation

SURGICAL PLANNING COMMUNICATION ▼
  • Report distance from tumor to major vessels (in mm)
  • Describe relationship: Displaced (patent) vs encased/compressed
  • Assess fat plane preservation
  • Note limb salvage feasibility
  • Identify optimal biopsy approach (avoid joint and neurovascular bundle)
