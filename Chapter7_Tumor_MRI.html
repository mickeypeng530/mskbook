Part I: General Principles

▼
1 Staging of Musculoskeletal Tumors ▼

MRI Role in Tumor Work-up

  • Confirm lesion presence
  • Allow specific diagnosis in some cases
  • Define tumor extent (local staging)
  • Guide biopsy planning
  • Evaluate recurrence after therapy

Three Components of Staging

Component Description Values
Grade (G) Metastatic Potential G1 = Low grade
G2 = High grade
Determined by biopsy/histology
Local Extent (T) Compartmental Involvement T1 = Intracompartmental
T2 = Extracompartmental
Metastases (M) Nodal/Distant Spread M0 = No metastases
M1 = Metastases present

Enneking Staging System

Stage Components Prognosis
IA G1 (Low) + T1 (Intracompartmental) + M0 Best
IB G1 (Low) + T2 (Extracompartmental) + M0 Good
IIA G2 (High) + T1 (Intracompartmental) + M0 Moderate
IIB G2 (High) + T2 (Extracompartmental) + M0 Poor
III Any G + Any T + M1 Worst

Extracompartmental Spread Mechanisms

  • Direct tumor invasion into adjacent compartment
  • Contamination from fracture or hemorrhage
  • Iatrogenic: unplanned resection or poorly planned biopsy
  • Each joint is a distinct compartment — articular invasion changes stage
2 Principles of Imaging ▼

Bone Tumors — Imaging Modalities

Modality Strengths Notes
Radiographs (XR) FIRST LINE, MOST SPECIFIC Essential even if MRI planned
MRI Most SENSITIVE, Best for LOCAL STAGING Appearance often nonspecific; MUST correlate with XR
Bone Scan Localize abnormality, Screen multifocal disease Can be negative with some lesions
CT Matrix characterization, Cortical destruction Better for calcification than MRI

Soft Tissue Tumors — Imaging

  • Radiographs still obtained first (may reveal bone involvement, calcifications)
  • MRI often allows specific diagnosis based on characteristic appearance
  • Even without specific diagnosis, MRI excellent for staging
3 Important MRI Features for Staging (Checklist) ▼
Feature Best Sequences Key Points
Intraosseous Extent T1W + STIR/FS-T2W STIR may overestimate (edema); T1 shows sharp contrast with fat
Extraosseous Extent T2W/STIR Edema: feathery margins, no mass effect
Neurovascular T1W axial + T2W Clear fat plane = no involvement
Joint Invasion T1W + T2W Each joint = separate compartment
Skip Metastases Large FOV STIR Non-contiguous foci in same bone
Local Adenopathy T1W + T2W Same poor prognosis as distant mets
📏 Measurement Reporting
  • A 5-cm "cuff" of normal tissue beyond tumor margins is desired at surgery
  • Provide exact measurements of intraosseous and extraosseous components
  • Reference to osseous landmark (e.g., "X cm from medial femoral condyle articular surface")
4 Evaluation of Tumor After Therapy ▼

Post-Chemotherapy Assessment

  • Responder: <10% viable tumor cells → Good prognosis
  • Non-responder: >10% viable tumor cells → Poor prognosis
  • Determined at histology after resection
⚠️ MRI Limitations
  • Changes in size, signal, or edema: NOT reliably predictive
  • Standard post-Gd T1W: Unreliable (both tumor AND reactive tissue enhance)
  • Dynamic enhancement: Residual tumor enhances faster than reactive tissue (technically challenging)

Post-Surgery/Radiation Assessment

Finding Interpretation
NO high T2/STIR signal Strong predictor of NON-RECURRENCE
High T2 signal + NO mass Likely edema/hemorrhage, NOT tumor
Normal "Texture Sign" Normal muscle architecture on T1W → Highly predictive of non-recurrence
Mass with peripheral-only enhancement Seroma, lymphocele, or abscess
Enhancing Mass RECURRENCE LIKELY → Biopsy indicated
5 How to Image Tumors — Standard Protocol ▼

Technical Setup

  • Position: Supine (standard); Prone rarely for sternum or comfort
  • Initial Sequence: Body coil, large FOV to ensure entire tumor identified
  • Detailed Imaging: Surface coil for higher resolution of margins, NV structures, joints
  • Skin Marker: Place over suspected mass; postop patients: image entire scar length

Pulse Sequence Protocol

# Sequence Plane FOV Purpose
1 STIR Cor/Sag Large Initial survey; sensitive for tumor, edema, skip lesions
2 T1W Cor/Sag Large Intraosseous extent; detect fat/hemorrhage
3 T1W Axial Small Tumor margins, anatomy, NV relationship
4 STIR/FS-T2W Axial Small Extraosseous extent, tissue characterization
5 T1W or FSE-T2W Sag/Cor Small Longitudinal plane; articular involvement
⚠️ FSE-T2W Pitfall

Fat remains bright on FSE-T2W → may mask intramedullary lesions

ALWAYS use fat saturation with FSE-T2W sequences for tumor imaging

Contrast Enhancement Guidelines

  • Standard tumor protocol: Gadolinium NOT routinely required
  • Cystic vs cystic-appearing solid: USE Gadolinium
  • Postoperative/postradiation follow-up: USE Gadolinium
  • Fat-sat post-Gd T1W: MUST obtain pre-contrast fat-sat T1W in SAME PLANE (to avoid pseudoenhancement)

6 Approach to Image Interpretation ▼

General Benign vs Malignant Features

Feature Typically Benign Typically Malignant
Margins Smooth, well-defined Irregular, ill-defined
Signal Homogeneous Heterogeneous
NV involvement Absent Present
Edema Minimal/absent Associated edema
⚠️ Critical Warning
  • SIGNIFICANT OVERLAP between benign and malignant lesion appearances
  • DANGEROUS to determine benign vs malignant based on MRI alone
  • Standard post-Gd T1W NOT helpful for differentiating benign vs malignant
  • Always correlate with radiographs — they provide the MOST SPECIFIC information

Aggressive-Appearing Benign Lesions

These benign lesions can display very aggressive MRI appearance due to associated edema:

Osteoid Osteoma
Location: Cortical
Key: Focal tumor NIDUS within cortical/periosteal reaction
Tip: CT often better for nidus visualization
Chondroblastoma
Location: EPIPHYSIS (skeletally immature patients)
Key: Striking edema extending to metaphysis and soft tissues
Osteoblastoma
Location: Spine (posterior elements)
Key: Larger than osteoid osteoma (>2cm); expansile
Eosinophilic Granuloma (LCH)
Location: Variable
Key: Langerhans cell histiocytosis; beveled edge on XR
Stress Fracture
Location: Weight-bearing bones
Key: Linear fracture line within edema
Follow-up: XR in 2-3 weeks if unclear

AVOID BIOPSY: immature osteoid may be mistaken for malignancy at histology

7 Bone Lesions with High T1 Signal ▼

High T1 signal indicates FAT or specific tissue compositions:

Intraosseous Lipoma
Location: Calcaneus, proximal femur, humerus
T1: HIGH (fat)
T2: Intermediate-High
Key: Predominant fat signal; may have central cystic change or calcification
Hemangioma (Simple - Spine)
Location: SPINE (very common)
T1: HIGH (fat content)
T2: HIGH
Key: "Polka-dot" appearance on axial; Aggressive type lacks fat
Medullary Bone Infarct
Location: Distal femur, proximal tibia
T1: HIGH (central fat preserved)
T2: Variable
Key: SERPENTINE LOW signal margin on all sequences; "double-line sign" on T2
Paget's Disease
Location: Pelvis, spine, skull, femur
T1: Variable (fat areas)
T2: Variable
Key: Cortical thickening, bone enlargement, coarse trabeculae; better on XR
🔍 Differentiating Lipoma vs Infarct
  • Lipoma: Well-defined, homogeneous fat signal, may have central calcification
  • Infarct: Geographic shape with SERPENTINE low-signal margin, central fat with heterogeneous foci
8 Bone Lesions with Low T2 Signal ▼

Low T2 signal suggests sclerosis, calcification, matrix, fibrous tissue:

Sclerosis/Calcification/Matrix
Examples: Bone island (enostosis), osteoid matrix
Key: Extremely low T2; better characterized with XR/CT
Xanthofibroma (NOF / Fibrous Cortical Defect)
Population: Adolescents/young adults
MRI: Intermediate-low T1, low-intermediate T2 (fibrous)
Key: Lobular contour, eccentric location, sclerotic margin
Tip: Easily diagnosed on radiographs
Fibrous Dysplasia
MRI: No characteristic appearance — heterogeneous signal
Key: May be high, low, or mixed T2 due to variable tissue
Tip: XR "ground glass" matrix often more diagnostic
Primary Lymphoma of Bone
Type: B-cell lymphoma
Key: Often LOW T2 (fibrosis); appearance varies
9 Fluid-Fluid Levels in Bone Lesions ▼

Fluid-fluid levels = stagnant blood products within cavernous spaces

Aneurysmal Bone Cyst (ABC) — Classic Appearance
  • Expansile, lobular mass
  • Multiple cyst-like collections with HIGH T2 signal
  • Fluid-fluid levels within cavities
  • Primary ABC: Non-enhancing spaces fill ENTIRE lesion

Differential Diagnosis of Fluid-Fluid Levels

Entity Nature Key Distinguishing Feature
Primary ABC Benign Non-enhancing spaces fill ENTIRE lesion
Telangiectatic Osteosarcoma Malignant Associated solid mass component
Giant Cell Tumor + Secondary ABC Aggressive benign Solid enhancing component present
Chondroblastoma Benign Epiphyseal location, skeletally immature
Fibrous Dysplasia Benign Ground-glass matrix on XR
MFH of Bone Malignant Aggressive features, older patient
📊 Fluid-Fluid Level Rule of Thirds
  • >2/3 of lesion = Usually BENIGN (often ABC)
  • <1/3 of lesion = Consider MALIGNANCY
  • Presence of associated SOLID MASS → Biopsy required
10 Cartilaginous Tumors ▼

Enchondroma — Classic MRI Features

Feature Appearance
Architecture Multiple lobules (hyaline cartilage)
T2 Signal Homogeneously HIGH within lobules
Internal Septa Thin, LOW signal (fibrous)
Low Signal Foci Stippled areas (calcified matrix)
Enhancement "Rings and Arcs" pattern
Surrounding Marrow NO surrounding edema (key benign feature)

Features Suggesting Chondrosarcoma (vs Enchondroma)

  • Deep endosteal scalloping (>2/3 cortex): Suggestive of malignancy
  • Cortical destruction ± soft tissue mass: Highly concerning
  • Edema in adjacent marrow or soft tissues: Worrisome
  • Size >5 cm: Larger lesions more likely malignant
  • Axial skeleton location: Higher risk than appendicular
⚠️ Cartilage Tumor Limitation

Often DIFFICULT or IMPOSSIBLE to distinguish enchondroma from low-grade chondrosarcoma

Same MRI appearance and enhancement pattern can be seen in both

Clinical correlation and sometimes biopsy required

Osteochondroma
Epidemiology: Most common benign bone tumor
Diagnosis: Usually on radiographs
MRI shows:
  • Continuity of lesion's medullary cavity and cortex with parent bone
  • Marrow fat isointense with host bone
  • HIGH T2 cartilage cap clearly visible
📏 Cartilage Cap Thickness

Cap >2 cm thickness: SUSPICIOUS for malignant degeneration (chondrosarcoma)

MRI can also show complications: neurovascular impingement, bursal formation, fracture

11 General Principles ▼
  • Some soft tissue tumors can be diagnosed with certainty based on MRI signal
  • LARGE OVERLAP in MRI appearance of benign and malignant masses
  • For indeterminate masses: use patient age, location, and differential tables
  • MOST indeterminate soft tissue masses should be BIOPSIED
📋 Differential Diagnosis Framework

For nonspecific MRI appearance, differential includes:

  • Top 3 benign tumors for given age and location
  • Top 3 malignant tumors for given age and location
  • Refer to distribution tables (Tables 7.2 and 7.3)
12 Soft Tissue Masses with High T1 Signal ▼

High T1 signal usually indicates: FAT or SUBACUTE BLOOD products

Lipoma (Benign)
T1: HIGH (fat)
T2: Intermediate-High
Key: Parallels subcutaneous fat ALL sequences; thin curvilinear septa
Morphology: Well-circumscribed (superficial) or infiltrative (intramuscular)
Atypical Lipoma / Well-Differentiated Liposarcoma
T1: Predominantly HIGH
T2: Variable
Key: Predominant fat with COARSE, THICKENED septa; scattered nonfatty tissue (low T1, high T2, enhancing)
Myxoid Liposarcoma
T1: LOW-Intermediate
T2: Very HIGH
Key: May appear CYSTIC; gelatinous; often NO recognizable fat
Hemangioma (Cavernous)
T1: Isointense to muscle + HIGH foci (fat)
T2: Very HIGH
Key: Lobular morphology; fat content; scattered LOW foci (phleboliths, thrombus)
Arteriovenous Malformation
T1: Flow voids + fat
T2: Flow voids
Key: Large high-flow vessels = DARK flow voids; HIGH on GRE
Hematoma (Subacute)
T1: HIGH (methemoglobin)
T2: Variable
Key: Heterogeneous, laminated; chronic = hemosiderin rim (low signal)
⚠️ Hematoma vs Hemorrhagic Tumor
  • Hemorrhagic neoplasm can be INDISTINGUISHABLE from traumatic hematoma
  • ANY hematoma must be followed to resolution
  • Gadolinium may show enhancing tumor mass, but organizing hematoma also enhances
  • If questionable: image-guided BIOPSY
💡 Fat-Containing Lesion

Gadolinium NOT necessary to diagnose fat-containing lesions (lipoma, atypical lipoma)

Fat saturation sequences confirm lipomatous nature

13 Soft Tissue Masses with Low T2 Signal ▼
PVNS (Pigmented Villonodular Synovitis)
T1: Intermediate-Low
T2: LOW (hemosiderin)
Location: Knee most common
Key: GRE: Characteristic "BLOOMING" of hemosiderin
DDx: Chronic hemarthrosis, RA, TB arthritis, amyloidosis, gout
Giant Cell Tumor of Tendon Sheath
T1: Intermediate-Low
T2: Heterogeneous, LOW foci
Key: Extra-articular PVNS; hand/wrist most common; adjacent to tendon
Fibrous Lesions
T1: Low-Intermediate
T2: Low-Intermediate
Examples: Plantar fibroma, Morton's neuroma, desmoid tumors
Amyloid
T1: Low-Intermediate
T2: Low-Intermediate
Location: Hip/shoulder most common
Association: Renal dialysis
Gout (Tophi)
T1: Low-Intermediate
T2: Low-Intermediate
Key: ± bone erosions; contains fibrous tissue, hemosiderin, or calcification
14 Cystic-Appearing Soft Tissue Masses ▼

True Cyst Features

  • T1 Signal: Homogeneous LOW (hypointense to muscle)
  • T2 Signal: Diffusely HIGH
  • Internal Septa: Thin, low signal (common in ganglion)
  • Post-Gd Enhancement: Thin peripheral wall and septa only; NO central enhancement

Differential Diagnosis

Entity Nature Enhancement Key Feature
Ganglion/Synovial Cyst Benign Peripheral only Typical locations (popliteal fossa, dorsal wrist)
Intramuscular Myxoma Benign Peripheral/septal; may have internal Well-circumscribed; gelatinous
Myxoid Liposarcoma Malignant Internal enhancement Solid tumor with myxoid elements
Myxoid Chondrosarcoma Malignant Internal enhancement Myxoid matrix
Synovial Sarcoma Malignant Diffuse internal Often appears cystic; enhancement proves solid
Nerve Sheath Tumor Usually benign Diffuse "Target sign"; split-fat sign; nerve entering/exiting
⚠️ Cystic-Appearing Mass Algorithm
  • If atypical location + absence of classic pure cyst features → USE GADOLINIUM
  • Internal enhancement → Solid component present → Consider BIOPSY
  • Even if cystic-appearing, atypical features warrant aspiration or biopsy

Nerve Sheath Tumor Features

  • Nerve Continuity: Tubular nerve entering and exiting mass
  • Split-Fat Sign: Peripheral rim of fat surrounding lesion
  • Internal Fascicles: Multiple small nerve fascicles within lesion on T2W
  • Target Sign: Higher signal periphery surrounding central lower signal on T2W
  • Enhancement: Diffuse enhancement after gadolinium

15 Structured Report Template for Tumor MRI ▼
  1. LESION IDENTIFICATION
    • Location (bone vs soft tissue, anatomic site)
    • Size (3 dimensions)
  2. SIGNAL CHARACTERISTICS
    • T1 signal (compared to muscle)
    • T2/STIR signal
    • Homogeneity
    • Fat content
    • Calcification/matrix
  3. MARGINS
    • Well-defined vs ill-defined
    • Capsule presence
  4. INTRAOSSEOUS EXTENT
    • Longitudinal extent with landmark reference
    • Example: "X cm from medial femoral condyle articular surface"
  5. EXTRAOSSEOUS EXTENT
    • Soft tissue mass dimensions
    • Compartment(s) involved
  6. NEUROVASCULAR STRUCTURES
    • Specific vessels/nerves identified
    • Clear fat plane vs encasement
    • If equivocal, state explicitly
  7. JOINT INVOLVEMENT
    • Distance from joint
    • Evidence of intra-articular extension
  8. SKIP LESIONS
    • Additional foci in same bone (non-contiguous with primary)
  9. LYMPHADENOPATHY
    • Local and regional nodes
  10. ENHANCEMENT PATTERN (if contrast given)
    • Homogeneous vs heterogeneous
    • Solid vs cystic
  11. IMPRESSION
    • Specific diagnosis if possible
    • Differential diagnosis
    • Staging summary
    • Recommendations
16 Summary of Diagnostic Pitfalls ▼
Pitfall Consequence Prevention
Relying on MRI alone for bone tumor characterization Misdiagnosis ALWAYS correlate with radiographs
Aggressive-appearing benign lesions Unnecessary aggressive treatment Know osteoid osteoma, chondroblastoma, stress fracture, LCH
Assuming benign vs malignant based on MRI margins Missed malignancy or over-treatment Significant overlap exists; biopsy when uncertain
Overestimating tumor extent on STIR Overcalling stage Correlate with T1W; edema ≠ tumor
Missing masked intramedullary lesions on FSE-T2 Missed tumor Use fat saturation with FSE-T2W
Pseudoenhancement False positive enhancement Obtain pre-contrast fat-sat T1W in SAME PLANE
Cystic-appearing malignancy Missed sarcoma Use Gd for atypical cystic masses
Not consulting anatomy atlas for NV structures Missed encasement if structures obliterated Always identify expected NV location
Biopsy of stress fracture Histology may mimic malignancy Follow with XR in 2-3 weeks instead
Confusing granulation tissue for recurrence False positive for recurrence Use texture sign; biopsy if uncertain
